# The Three-Body Problem: Israel, Iran, and the United States

---

> **⚠ Core Thesis**
>
> The original *Empty Pipeline* report framed this crisis around a bilateral US-Iran standoff with three probabilistic outcomes. That framing is now structurally incomplete. A third actor — Israel — is pursuing objectives that are categorically incompatible with the terms Iran requires to close any deal. This creates a **three-body problem**: three actors with irreconcilable gravitational pulls, where achieving any one actor's objectives necessarily prevents another's. Scenario A (Ceasefire/Partial Reopening) as originally described is not merely unlikely — it is geometrically impossible under current conditions without a fundamental change in Israeli operational posture that Netanyahu has given no indication of accepting.

---

## Part I: The Three Actors and Their Incompatible Objectives

### 1. The United States: Time as the Binding Constraint

The US entered this conflict with two stated objectives — eliminating Iran's nuclear capability and reopening the Strait of Hormuz — but a third, unstated objective now dominates all decision-making: **extracting before the midterms**.

Trump's approval on the economy has collapsed to 29% in Reuters/Ipsos polling — worse than Biden's worst numbers — with 66% of Americans disapproving of his handling of gas prices. His pollster Tony Fabrizio has conducted surveys showing the war is growing increasingly unpopular. White House aides have warned him explicitly that the longer the war drags on, the more it threatens Republican prospects in November.

The time horizon this creates is measured in **weeks, not months**. Trump needs oil below $4/gallon at the pump before early summer for any economic recovery to register in polling. The Islamabad talks have a two-week window. Extensions of that window carry diminishing political returns. This is not a party with strategic patience.

The behavioral pattern is established and already executing: escalate rhetorically to the edge, accept a face-saving off-ramp dressed as victory, declare "total and complete success," and rely on the media cycle to move on. It worked — briefly — with the April 7 ceasefire announcement. It failed within hours when Israel launched its largest strikes on Lebanon of the entire war.

**Trump's objective:** A deal — any deal — he can brand as victory before summer.

**Time horizon:** 2–6 weeks.

---

### 2. Iran: The Structural Demand That Cannot Be Conceded

Iran's strategic position is more coherent than Western analysis typically credits. They lost their supreme leader (Khamenei was killed in the February 28 strikes), absorbed the most intensive air campaign since the Gulf War, and watched their nuclear facilities damaged. Yet they retained the one asset that matters: **physical control of the Strait of Hormuz**.

That control is not leverage — it *is* the negotiating position. Iran's 10-point framework, viewed in this light, is not an opening bid. It is a statement of minimum requirements:

- Cessation of hostilities across **all fronts**, specifically including Lebanon
- Controlled (not free) passage through the Strait, with Iran retaining coordination authority
- Full sanctions relief and frozen asset release
- Reparations for war damages
- No enrichment prohibition

Each of these terms is structurally load-bearing. The Lebanon demand in particular is not negotiable for Tehran: Hezbollah is Iran's primary deterrent against future Israeli unilateral strikes and its most powerful regional proxy. Allowing Hezbollah to be systematically destroyed while Iran sits at a negotiating table would mean accepting the elimination of Iran's regional influence architecture — the very thing they fought to preserve.

There is also a domestic political dimension. Hard-liners in Tehran are already burning flags and chanting "Death to compromisers" after the ceasefire announcement. Any deal that abandons Lebanon while Iran is simultaneously claiming victory would be politically untenable for the Pezeshkian government.

**Iran's objective:** A deal that preserves Hezbollah as a functional force, retains Iranian influence over Hormuz passage, and provides economic reconstruction. Alternatively: continued attrition until those terms are accepted.

**Time horizon:** Months to years. Iran has demonstrated strategic patience across decades of sanctions. The two-week ceasefire window imposes no pressure on them comparable to what it imposes on Trump.

---

### 3. Israel: The Third Body — Territorial Reconfiguration as Existential Strategy

This is the actor the original framework did not fully account for, and it is the one that makes Scenario A structurally impossible in its original formulation.

Israel's objectives in this conflict are **categorically different** from those of both other actors. They are not primarily about the strait, oil prices, nuclear enrichment timelines, or diplomatic resolution. They are about **permanent territorial reconfiguration** of Israel's northern security environment — a generational strategic objective that Netanyahu has been explicit about:

> "I said we would change the face of the Middle East, and we have done so. But we have also changed our security concept. We initiate, we attack, and we have created three security zones deep within enemy territory — in Syria, Gaza, and Lebanon."

The historical lesson Israel's security establishment has drawn from the 2000 withdrawal, the 2006 war, the 2023 attacks, and the March 2026 Hezbollah rocket campaign is consistent: negotiated ceasefire lines without physical Israeli presence do not prevent Hezbollah from reconstituting. The November 2024 ceasefire was violated — by Hezbollah's rebuilding, by near-daily Israeli strikes — and within 15 months, the rocket campaign resumed. Israel has concluded it cannot rely on Lebanese state authority, UN resolutions, or paper agreements to protect its northern population.

The response is the "Gaza model" applied to southern Lebanon: create facts on the ground, establish buffer zones through military force, render the terrain ungovernable for Hezbollah, and negotiate political legitimacy for those facts afterward with US backing.

**Operationally, this means:**

- A permanent Israeli military presence up to the Litani River (15–20 miles inside Lebanon)
- Continued air operations against Hezbollah infrastructure in Beirut and the Bekaa Valley regardless of diplomatic calendars
- Full disarmament of Hezbollah as the political objective, even if the military means have been recalibrated to a "prolonged process"

**Israel's objective:** Permanent security zone in southern Lebanon; Hezbollah degraded to the point of strategic irrelevance; no external ceasefire agreement that constrains Israeli freedom of action in Lebanon.

**Time horizon:** Measured in **years**. Israel's military planners have explicitly said they expect the Lebanon campaign to outlast the Iran campaign. There is no Israeli midterm election, no collapsing approval rating, no gas price political crisis. Netanyahu's political survival actually depends on *continuing* the campaign — his coalition includes Smotrich's faction, which is calling for the Litani River as Israel's new border.

## Part II: Why the Three Bodies Cannot Be Reconciled

The collision is not diplomatic — it is geometric. Each actor's minimum requirements directly prevent another's:

**Iran requires Lebanon be included in any ceasefire. Israel requires Lebanon be excluded.**

This is not a negotiating gap. It is a binary incompatibility. Pakistan's Prime Minister Sharif announced the ceasefire explicitly included Lebanon. Netanyahu announced within hours that it did not, and immediately launched the largest single-day strikes of the entire war on Beirut — killing over 250 people on the day of the ceasefire announcement. Iran responded by closing the strait again.

The US position — that Lebanon is "a separate skirmish" (Trump) and that Iran would be "dumb" to let Lebanon derail negotiations (Vance) — is analytically coherent from Washington's perspective but irrelevant to Tehran's strategic calculus. Hezbollah is not a "separate skirmish" from Iran's perspective; it is the cornerstone of the resistance architecture that justifies the entire cost of the war.

**The deal structure that Trump can accept is one Iran cannot sign. The deal Iran can sign is one Israel will not allow Trump to enforce.**

This creates a specific and underappreciated dynamic: **Israel has unilateral veto power over the ceasefire without being a formal party to the US-Iran negotiations.** Every major Israeli strike on Lebanon triggers Iranian strait responses, which restarts the oil shock, which destroys the economic rationale for Trump's off-ramp. Israel does not need to formally oppose the deal — it simply needs to continue its declared operational objectives in Lebanon.

## Part III: Revised Scenario Analysis

The original three-scenario framework requires substantive revision. The scenarios are reframed below to account for the three-body dynamic.

---

### Revised Scenario A: Partial Functional Resolution (~15%)
*Previously assessed at 35%*

**What would need to happen:** The US successfully pressures Israel to pause Lebanon operations for the duration of negotiations (not permanently — just pause). Iran accepts a deal that does not formally include Lebanon cessation but includes implicit understandings via intermediaries. The strait reopens under coordinated passage protocols. Both sides declare victory on their own terms.

**Why the probability is lower than the original assessment:** This requires Netanyahu to accept operational constraints in Lebanon that his coalition will not support and that contradict his stated security doctrine. The political cost to Netanyahu of pausing Lebanon operations — loss of Smotrich and Ben-Gvir's coalition support — is existential for his government. The US has limited leverage here: Trump has already praised Netanyahu's cooperation publicly, removing the threat of US diplomatic pressure as a credible coercive tool.

**Price implication:** Brent retreats toward $80–90. The prediction market's ~73% probability on WTI above $115 by year-end would be repriced sharply downward. This is the scenario that explains the Goldman Sachs Q4 $60/bbl base case — it assumes a clean resolution that the three-body analysis suggests is structurally improbable.

**Oil price by year-end if this scenario holds:** WTI $75–95.

---

### Revised Scenario B: Managed Fragmentation (~50%)
*Previously assessed at 45% — probability maintained but character substantially changed*

**What this looks like now:** The US-Iran ceasefire holds in a narrow technical sense — attacks on Iran's territory are paused — but Israel continues Lebanon operations, Iran continues fee-based strait access rather than full reopening, and a permanent bilateral deal remains out of reach through the two-week window and multiple subsequent extensions. The situation becomes a "frozen conflict" where no party formally breaks the ceasefire but no party achieves its objectives.

**The key mechanism:** Iran institutionalizes the transit fee system for the strait — charging vessels for coordinated passage through its armed forces. This is qualitatively different from the original Scenario B. It does not represent a return to the pre-war status quo of free international passage. It represents Iran permanently monetizing its control over global energy transit. Gulf Arab states, which also depend on strait access for their own exports, cannot accept this long-term — setting up a second-order conflict dynamic.

**The financial transmission:** Under this scenario, oil oscillates in a structurally elevated band. Every Israeli strike on Lebanon carries the tail risk of Iranian strait closure. Markets cannot price this as "resolved" because it isn't — it's managed fragmentation, and the management can break down on any given day.

**Oil price by year-end if this scenario holds:** WTI $100–130, with frequent spikes above and below on news events.

---

### Revised Scenario C: Cascading Breakdown (~35%)
*Previously assessed at 20% — probability materially increased*

**The upgraded probability reflects the three-body mechanism:** Scenario C no longer requires a deliberate choice by any party to escalate. It can occur through the **automatic collision** of Israeli operations and Iranian red lines, independent of US-Iran diplomatic progress.

**Trigger chains:**
- Israel strikes Hezbollah assets in Beirut's Dahiyeh district → Iran closes strait → US demands reopening → Iran re-closes → oil spikes above $130 → Treasury yields breach 5% → sovereign debt servicing stress → financial contagion begins
- Or: Israeli strike damages Iranian oil infrastructure in southern Lebanon or Gulf state allied facilities → Iran expands mining of the strait beyond current pattern → insurance markets withdraw from Gulf shipping entirely → physical supply chain breakdown exceeds strategic reserve capacity

**The stagflationary Minsky intersection:** This is where the debt analysis from the project files becomes directly relevant. A Scenario C event does not produce the deflationary Minsky dynamic of 2008 — it produces the stagflationary variant: essential goods (energy, food) inflate sharply while credit simultaneously tightens. Central banks face the impossible choice between fighting inflation (raise rates, accelerate sovereign debt crisis on $50T+ in G7 debt) or supporting growth (cut rates, feed inflation). Fisher's debt-deflation theory assumed falling prices as the mechanism; the current configuration may produce equally destabilizing dynamics through the opposite channel.

**Oil price by year-end if this scenario holds:** WTI $140–$200, consistent with the prediction market's 32¢ ($150+) and 16¢ ($200+) contracts.

## Part IV: The Israel Variable as an Ongoing Oil Price Trigger

The most important analytical update for ongoing monitoring is this: **Israeli military operations in Lebanon are now a direct, repeatable oil price mechanism.**

The causal chain is demonstrated and has already activated once:

1. Israel strikes Hezbollah targets in Lebanon
2. Iran invokes Lebanon's inclusion in ceasefire terms and closes the strait
3. Oil spikes $10–15/barrel within hours
4. Markets partially recover on diplomatic statements, then re-price on the next strike cycle

This mechanism operates **independently of the US-Iran negotiating track**. Even a successful Islamabad negotiation framework does not disable it. As long as Israel continues Lebanon operations — which Netanyahu has stated unequivocally it will — and as long as Iran maintains that Lebanon is a ceasefire condition — which its 10-point framework explicitly states — the strait remains functionally unstable.

## Part V: What Has Not Changed — The Physical Reality

None of the three-body diplomatic complexity alters the physical supply facts documented in the original *Empty Pipeline* report. The Rong Lin Wan delivered the last pre-blockade jet fuel shipment to Rotterdam today. The spring fertilizer window has closed. The buffer exhaustion is complete.

These consequences are now **locked in regardless of diplomatic outcomes**:

- European aviation fuel shortfall materializes in April–May 2026 under any scenario
- 2026 Northern Hemisphere grain and vegetable yields reduced by fertilizer shortage — food price inflation in H2 2026 is baked in
- Refinery crude slate mismatches (Gulf heavy/sour vs. available light/sweet) will persist for months even after strait reopening
- US shale response: EIA projects only 20,000 bpd additional output for 2026; meaningful response deferred to late 2026–2027

The strait can reopen tomorrow and the food inflation still hits. The aviation crisis still materializes. The financial system is absorbing an oil shock that the physical supply chain cannot recover from quickly regardless of what happens in Islamabad.

## Part VI: Integration with the Debt and Regulatory Cycle Framework

The three-body problem sits directly on top of the conditions described in *Debt & Leverage as Crisis Catalysts v2.0* — and the interaction effects are the critical underappreciated risk.

**The regulatory cycle position:** Per the *Regulatory Cycle Phases* framework, the financial system is currently in late Phase 4 active deregulation — shock absorbers at their thinnest since 2007. CFPB enforcement capacity reduced. Enhanced capital requirement thresholds raised. Derivatives oversight weakened. This is the moment of maximum financial system fragility coinciding with the maximum geopolitical and commodity shock.

**The Minsky-Hormuz transmission:** The commodity shock creates inflationary pressure while simultaneously tightening credit. The Brunnermeier liquidity spiral mechanism (cited in the debt analysis) can activate through the commodity channel rather than the credit channel — oil companies facing margin calls, energy ETFs experiencing redemptions, sovereign wealth funds in Gulf states drawing down portfolios to fund domestic economic support. The financial crisis, if it comes, will not look like 2008's deflationary collapse. It will look like stagflation with a credit crunch embedded in it.

**The debt servicing trap:** G7 sovereign debt exceeds 100% of GDP across all member states simultaneously. The 10-year Treasury yield hit 4.46% on March 27. Every 50 basis points of rate increase adds approximately $250 billion annually in debt servicing costs across G7 governments. A sustained oil shock that keeps CPI elevated forces central banks to hold rates, which feeds into sovereign debt stress, which amplifies the financial crisis risk the debt framework identifies as the background condition of this entire period.

## Conclusion: Scenario A Requires a Deus Ex Machina

The original *Empty Pipeline* report was directionally correct but did not fully account for the three-body dynamic. Scenario A — clean ceasefire, partial reopening, price retreat — required only two parties to agree. The three-body problem means it now requires three parties to agree, including one (Israel) that has no political incentive to agree, no economic pressure to agree, and has stated explicitly that its objectives in Lebanon will continue regardless of US-Iran diplomacy.

Scenario A is not impossible. It would require either a fundamental shift in Netanyahu's coalition calculus (possible if his government falls), explicit and credible US military pressure on Israel to pause Lebanon operations (historically unprecedented and politically impossible for Trump given his base), or a unilateral Hezbollah stand-down that Israel accepts as sufficient (unlikely given the IDF's stated goal of permanent buffer zones).

Absent one of those conditions, the most probable path is the **revised Scenario B**: a frozen, fragmented partial resolution that keeps oil structurally elevated, keeps strait access conditional and fee-bearing, and keeps the Scenario C trigger loaded — with Israel's operational tempo as the detonator.

The prediction market's implied distribution — 73% probability of WTI above $115 by year-end, 51% above $130, 32% above $150 — is not paranoid. Given the three-body geometry described above, it may still be underpricing the tail.
