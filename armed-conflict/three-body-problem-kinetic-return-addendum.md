# The Three-Body Problem — Kinetic-Return Addendum: The Spoiler Comes Back to the Bases

---

> **Scope note**
>
> This is the **second** implementation-phase addendum to the April 9 three-body analysis, and it is a sequel to the June 24 first addendum (*Process Spoilers and the Contested 60-Day Clock*). It is an addendum, not a replacement: the original three-actor geometry — Israel, Iran, and the United States, with the Hezbollah "fourth body" coupling Lebanon to Hormuz — still holds and is not re-litigated. What this document updates is the *channel* through which the incompatibility now expresses itself. The first addendum argued that the spoiler had migrated from the **kinetic** channel the April report modeled to a **process** channel — INARA mechanics and nuclear-verification obstruction, "the collision surface moved from the Litani River to the text of a memorandum." The June 27–28 sequence **partially falsifies that**: the spoiler vector has returned to direct kinetics, at a new theater. This addendum documents the channel switch back — and the more important finding sitting on top of it, that the financial-market transmission the whole project tracks did not follow the violence.

---

## Part I: What Happened — The Channel Switched Back to Kinetics

The first addendum's central claim was a migration: the irreconcilable three-body geometry had stopped expressing itself through ordnance and started expressing itself through process — the IAEA-access dispute, the loaded-but-unfired INARA lever, the contested 60-day clock. That claim was correct for the June 21–24 window. It does not survive June 27–28. Two events returned the spoiler to the kinetic channel and moved its theater from the maritime corridor to GCC military hubs.

### (a) June 27 — A second vessel, and the first targeting of the closure-trigger infrastructure

A *second* commercial vessel was struck within 72 hours: the **Kiku**, a Panama-flagged VLCC (~300,866 dwt) reportedly carrying ~2M bbl of Qatari crude, hit by an IRGC attack drone in the Strait. The damage was minor — bridge damage, **no casualties, no sinking**, the vessel remained seaworthy and continued transit (CENTCOM attribution, **Tier 1 — CONFIRMED**). Two qualitative escalators distinguish this from the June 25 *Ever Lovely* strike:

- **The target may have been the MOU's operational mechanism, not opportunistic tonnage.** The Kiku was reportedly struck *on the designated safe corridor* — the IMO-backed transit route that is the implementation framework's own safety mechanism. If confirmed, this is the kinetic arena attacking the deal's operating system rather than a passing ship. **This attribution is Tier 3 (TechTimes) only and is flagged REPORTED-UNVERIFIED pending Windward/UKMTO Tier 1/2 confirmation.** It is load-bearing for the "attack on implementation itself" reading and should not be stated as established until firmer-sourced.
- **The US target set now connects to the L4 closure language for the first time.** The US ran a *second night* of strikes on Iranian military targets, the set now expanded to **minelayer capabilities** — i.e., the US is pre-emptively striking the *mining-enablement infrastructure* that underlies the Level 4 "Strait of Hormuz physical infrastructure struck, mined, or formally closed" trigger. This is the first time that staged trigger language connects to an actual targeting event. It did not fire the trigger — no mining occurred and no infrastructure was struck — but it moves the kinetic activity adjacent to the threshold the project watches most closely.

### (b) June 28 — Ballistic missiles at two US bases

On June 28 Iran fired ballistic missiles at **Ali Al Salem Air Base (Kuwait)** and **US Fifth Fleet HQ / NSA Bahrain (Juffair)** — the first direct Iran-on-US-base attack of the war and a categorical step-change from the vessel-strike-and-claim pattern. The verification discipline matters here, because the temptation is to read the Iranian framing as the event:

- **Real ballistic missiles launched and intercepted — CONFIRMED** (Kuwait government, Tier 1 ally: two detected and intercepted, no casualties).
- **Partial penetration of the Bahrain area — CONFIRMED** (Bahrain Interior Ministry, Tier 1: a residential building near the airport damaged, no fatalities — civilian proximity, not military infrastructure).
- **No damage to US forces — CONFIRMED** (CENTCOM, Tier 1).
- **The IRGC "8 US infrastructure targets destroyed" claim — REPORTED-UNVERIFIED** (Iranian state media; CENTCOM actively denies). Iran *claims* eight targets destroyed; that is the only honest framing.

What is confirmed, then, is narrower but still categorical: real missiles, launched at US installations, in two GCC countries, intercepted by one Tier 1 ally with confirmed civilian-proximity damage in another — and a CENTCOM that *actively contests the outcome* rather than ignoring it. That active contestation is itself informative: it is materially different from the June 26 ISNA counter-strike claim, which CENTCOM met with silence (and which was therefore not logged as fact). When CENTCOM bothers to deny, the missiles were real and aimed at US installations.

### (c) The L4 discipline — what did *not* happen

This addendum was released on **geometry grounds, by user adjudication** — not because a Level 4 trigger fired. None did, and the precision is load-bearing:

- No Strait of Hormuz **physical infrastructure** strike, mining, or UNCLOS-enforced closure. A vessel is not infrastructure; a struck minelayer-capability target on Iranian soil is not the strait being mined.
- No nuclear or radiological event; the strikes were on coastal/military targets, not nuclear sites.
- The one staged trigger that *could* have made this a confirmed new-geometry escalation — a **CENTCOM-confirmed strike on US forces** — was **literally unmet**: CENTCOM reported no force damage. The release does not rest on that trigger. It rests on the *categorical* change in the spoiler's channel and theater (direct ballistic missiles at US bases plus a second corridor vessel strike), which the escalation tree recognizes as a "new variable" warranting an L3 update to an existing analysis. The original three-body framing still holds, so this is an addendum, not a full report.

## Part II: The Geometry Shift — From the Memorandum Back to a Missile

The first addendum located the collision surface inside the text of a memorandum. The June 27–28 sequence moves it back to a missile — but at a theater the April analysis did not specifically model. The shift has three components.

**The channel reverted.** The process spoiler (IAEA dispute, INARA) has not disappeared, but it is no longer where the action is. The deal's stress is again being applied kinetically — a vessel strike on the corridor and ballistic missiles at bases — rather than through verification mechanics or legislative noise. The first addendum's "spoiler changed channels, not targets" thesis is, for this window, inverted: the spoiler changed back to the kinetic channel *and* changed targets.

**The theater moved.** The April report's kinetic geometry ran through two theaters — Lebanon (Israeli strikes) and Hormuz (Iranian strait responses), coupled through the Hezbollah fourth body. June 27–28 opens a **third kinetic node: GCC basing hubs**. Iran striking Ali Al Salem and Juffair is a direct state-on-state exchange at US installations — kinetics no longer confined to the maritime corridor or the Lebanon front. This is genuinely new geometry: the April analysis modeled Iran's leverage as *strait control*, exercised against shipping; it did not model Iran firing ballistic missiles at allied basing infrastructure as a deliberate, survivable signaling act inside an implementation window.

**The original incompatibility is intact underneath.** None of this reconciles the three bodies. The structural collision the April report identified — Iran's minimum terms versus Israel's Lebanon doctrine, mediated by a US racing a political clock — is unchanged. What June 27–28 shows is that the parties can prosecute that collision through a new kinetic theater while the formal 60-day Bürgenstock roadmap remains, on paper, un-repudiated. The geometry escalated; it did not transform. That is precisely why this is an addendum.

## Part III: The Market Decoupling — The Richest Finding

Through the entire sequence above — a second vessel struck, US minelayer-targeting strikes, ballistic missiles at two US bases — **Brent fell to ~$72 and kept falling**, down roughly 10% on the week. This is not a footnote to the escalation; it is the finding.

The throughput data also supplies the *measurable fingerprint* of the channel switch this addendum documents. During the post-signing window of June 22–26, strait transits had **recovered to ~44/day (~33% of normal, single-day peak ~49 on June 24)** — the deal, briefly, pulling tonnage back into the corridor. Then, after the Kiku strike and the IRGC corridor-enforcement directive ("AIS off, or off-route, and you carry the consequences"), transits **collapsed roughly 70% to ~13/day by June 29**. That 44→13 reversal is the physical signature of the kinetic channel pushing the strait number back down — the empirical form of "the spoiler came back to the bases." (Caveat: the June 29 figure is a single-day Windward read — directional, not yet 24h-confirmed.) The decisive point is that this reversal shows up in (A), the strait transit count, while (C), total outflow, held its 33–45% band throughout: the corridor closed, the oil did not. The addendum must hold two simultaneous truths without collapsing one into the other: the **geometry escalated** (kinetics returned, new theater, ballistic missiles on US bases) while the **financial-market transmission decoupled** (oil priced de-escalation *through* accelerating violence). The release-valve thesis the project has tracked since the spring is now confirmed at near-extreme.

**The widening that did happen is not Iran-risk.** The one cross-asset move in the window — HY OAS widening +20bp (June 17→26, 2.63%→2.83%, FRED Tier 1) — was tested against the Iran channel and resolves to **rate-path repricing, not geopolitical risk**. The driver is BofA's June 22 three-hike call ("inflation getting unambiguously worse"); the widening tracks the Fed-path story already logged (the Warsh hawkish dots, June 19), not the kinetics. The tell is direction: Brent fell *through* the same window the HY spread widened. No credit event occurred. The "HY-tight as structural disqualifier" condition weakens only marginally — 2.83% sits far below the 3.5% break level and the 5.0% WARN — and remains operative. VIX at 18.4 and the 10-year at 4.38 (falling) corroborate: there is no risk-premium repricing of the Iran kinetics anywhere in the cross-asset tape.

**Why the decoupling holds.** Two non-exclusive mechanisms account for an oil market that shrugged off ballistic missiles at US bases:

1. **The STS workaround is absorbing the flow.** The release-valve thesis from the first addendum held that the strait's strategic value as Iranian leverage has partially migrated *outside the strait itself* — to ship-to-ship transfer anchorages off Fujairah and Sohar and to pipelines that route crude around the chokepoint. A throughput monitor now puts hard numbers on that migration, and the numbers are the empirical backbone of the two-truths finding. The critical distinction is between the transit figure headlines cite and the total-outflow figure that actually governs price:

   - **(A) AIS strait transits ran ~13/day on June 29 — roughly 10–14% of the pre-war baseline** (Windward puts that baseline at 130–138/day; **Tier 2, CONFIRMED**). This is the number that looks like near-total closure.
   - **(C) Total oil outflow, including the workaround, is ~6.5–9 mb/d — roughly 33–45% of the 20 mb/d pre-war baseline** (**REPORTED-UNVERIFIED, composite estimate**). This is the number that governs Brent, and it has *never* fallen below ~20% through the entire war. Its components: STS off Fujairah/Sohar at ~1.5–2 mb/d (≥90M bbl transferred since early May, US-Navy-monitored; **Tier 2, but the figure runs only through June 17 — a 12-day-stale read**); the Habshan–Fujairah pipeline at ~1.1–1.5 mb/d and the Petroline / East-West line to the Red Sea at ~2–3 mb/d, **both bypassing the strait entirely** (**CONFIRMED at floor**; any Petroline ramp above its ~2 mb/d floor is unconfirmed).

   The pipeline-only bypass is a **structural ~15–20% floor that holds even if strait transits go to zero**, and it sits entirely outside IRGC corridor reach. The named falsifier for the whole thesis is a **kinetic break of the STS anchorages** (or the pipelines). As of this pass that falsifier **remains un-tripped**: the June 25–28 strikes hit vessels *on the AIS corridor*, never the Gulf-of-Oman STS anchorages or the bypass pipelines. So long as (C) holds while (A) collapses, corridor kinetics do not translate into supply loss, and the oil tape can stay quiet through escalation. (Note: the Kpler ~4.8 mb/d strait-volume figure is a June 13–21, *pre-Kiku* read and must not be used as a current strait number.)
2. **The market reads the exchange as controlled theater inside an intact roadmap.** No party has formally repudiated the 60-day Bürgenstock roadmap. The tit-for-tat — Iranian vessel strike, US retaliation, Iranian missiles met by CENTCOM "no force damage," oil falling — is being priced as managed signaling inside a strained-but-standing framework rather than as the onset of Scenario C. The discipline of the exchange (interceptions, civilian-proximity-only damage, no force casualties, no infrastructure struck) supports that reading.

**Brent at $72 tracks (C), not (A).** The price is reconcilable once the workaround floor is priced in: (C)'s ~6.5 mb/d floor, plus IEA reserve releases (~4.1 mb/d), plus demand destruction left over from the April $138 spike, plus the Bürgenstock deal functioning as a market put. $72 is what a ~33–45% total-outflow world with a structural pipeline floor and a standing roadmap looks like — not what a ~10–14% strait-transit world would look like if the strait were the whole story. The single thing that would break $72 is the falsifier tripping: a strike on the Gulf-of-Oman STS anchorages or the bypass pipelines. Through June 28 that has not happened.

The honest synthesis is the paradox itself: **the collision surface moved back to a missile, but the oil tape didn't follow it there.** The project tracks the financial-market transmission of geopolitical shock; in this window the transmission went quiet precisely as the shock intensified. That is not a contradiction to resolve away — it is the single most important observation of the pass, and it is the strongest release-valve confirmation in the record to date. The standing caveat: a decoupling this complete is itself a risk object. If the STS workaround is struck, or the roadmap is formally repudiated, the re-coupling could be abrupt — the market is currently pricing a level of containment that a single anchorage strike could falsify.

## Conclusion: The Surface Moved Back to a Missile — and the Tape Didn't Follow

The first addendum said the collision surface had moved from the Litani River to the text of a memorandum. This one documents the surface moving from the memorandum back to a missile — a second VLCC struck on what may be the deal's own safe corridor, US strikes reaching the mining-enablement infrastructure under the L4 closure trigger, and Iranian ballistic missiles at two US bases in two GCC countries. The spoiler returned to direct kinetics, at a theater the April analysis did not model. The geometry escalated.

But the financial-market transmission the project exists to track went quiet at exactly that moment. Brent fell to ~$72 and kept falling through the most kinetically intense week of the implementation phase; the only cross-asset widening was rate-path repricing, not Iran-risk; and the named release-valve falsifier — a strike on the STS anchorages — remains un-tripped. The market is pricing the exchange as controlled theater inside an intact, if strained, 60-day roadmap.

The three bodies are no closer to reconciliation than they were on April 9 or June 24. What June 27–28 establishes is that they can now prosecute their irreconcilable collision through a third kinetic theater — and that, for now, the oil tape will not price it. The paradox is now literally quantified: the strait transit count went 44→13 while total outflow held its 33–45% band. The geometry came back to the missile. The market stayed at the memorandum.
