# April 15 Addendum: Chinese Military-Channel Transit Declaration; Interdiction Ambiguity

---

## Overview

The April 14 addendum forecast a specific watch list indicator as imminent: "Chinese/Indian vessel attempting Hormuz transit under bilateral deal; US interdiction orders tested." The addendum specified India as the higher-probability near-term actor based on visible domestic pressure (LPG crisis, mass worker displacement) and identified the April 21 ceasefire expiry as the next hard binary driving the pressure clock. Two things about that forecast require update within 24 hours.

First, the indicator fired — but the actor order was inverted. China moved before India, and the move came through the military channel (Defense Minister Dong Jun) rather than the diplomatic channel (Foreign Ministry). Second, a tier-1 reporting gap on the blockade's first 24 hours has revealed an interdiction loophole that Iran-linked vessels are already exploiting, with the Pentagon/CENTCOM position materially at odds with Reuters and Al Jazeera vessel-tracking reports.

## Section 1: The Forecast Was Correct; The Actor Order Was Not

The Three Blockade Problem's indicator table (April 12) names "vessels attempt transit under bilateral deal; US interdiction orders tested" as the primary escalation signal for Chinese/Indian vessel behavior and the principal Scenario D catalyst. The April 14 addendum assessed that indicator as imminent and specified India as the more likely near-term actor, anchoring the pressure clock to the April 21 ceasefire expiry. The indicator fired within 24 hours, and it fired six days ahead of that anchor.

Two features of the fire pattern warrant documentation.

### The actor was China, not India

India's domestic pressure clock (LPG black market, 150,000+ displaced textile workers from Surat, 500,000+ impacted across Gujarat manufacturing, OFAC waiver expiry) remains fully intact as documented April 14. None of that pressure has been relieved. India was the higher-probability near-term actor because the domestic mechanism was visible; China's calculus was characterized as governed by strategic patience and the option to let India absorb initial interdiction risk.

That characterization did not account for China treating the blockade's first 24 hours as the window of maximum US uncertainty — the moment before enforcement precedent hardens. Moving first, before India or any other actor tests the line, maximizes Chinese leverage over the shape of whatever enforcement norm emerges. India remains a secondary catalyst whose pressure clock continues to run; China has simply moved to the leading edge.

### The channel was military, not diplomatic

The April 14 logged signal was Chinese Foreign Ministry — a diplomatic register ("dangerous and irresponsible," plus the general declaration of continued transit intent). The April 15 signal is Defense Minister Dong Jun stating: "We have trade and energy agreements with Iran; we expect others not to interfere in our affairs. The Strait of Hormuz is open to us" (Chinese Defense Ministry, 2026). This is a material escalation on the channel dimension. A Foreign Ministry statement is a diplomatic posture open to subsequent calibration; a Defense Minister declaration that the strait is "open to us" is an operational-command statement that commits the PLA chain of command to the position and binds any subsequent climbdown to visible military retraction.

The watch list indicator is framed around whether vessels attempt transit and the US interdiction order is tested. Both conditions are now active simultaneously. Sanctioned Iran-linked tankers have transited Hormuz during the blockade's first 24–48 hours (Al Jazeera, 2026; Reuters, 2026). China's Defense Ministry has stated publicly that Chinese continued use of the strait is not negotiable. The signal has crossed from forecast to confirmed.

---

## Section 2: The Interdiction Ambiguity

The April 15 reporting presents a direct tier-1-versus-tier-1 disagreement on a load-bearing fact. The Pentagon stated that no ships "made it past" the US blockade in its first 24 hours and that six merchant ships were turned back (Pentagon/CENTCOM, 2026). Al Jazeera and Reuters separately reported that at least three vessels transited the strait during the same window, and by April 15 at least four Iran-linked ships had crossed (Al Jazeera, 2026; Reuters, 2026).

This is not resolvable as a simple factual contradiction, because CENTCOM clarified the operative definition of the blockade: it applies to vessels entering or leaving Iranian ports. Vessels routing to third-party destinations — even when Iran-linked or carrying sanctioned cargo — fall outside the stated enforcement scope. The Pentagon's "no ships passed" and Reuters' "at least three transited" can both be literally accurate under that clarification, because the two sources are counting different denominators.

The policy content of the clarification is the signal. A port-destination enforcement rule creates a structural loophole that an Iran-linked vessel can satisfy by declaring a non-Iranian transshipment destination — a widely documented practice in the pre-blockade sanctions regime. The CENTCOM clarification converts the blockade from a strait-closure posture into a port-of-call filter, which is a materially weaker enforcement instrument than Trump's April 12 declaration ("all ships entering or exiting the Strait") implied.

The deeper signal is what the clarification declines to say. As of April 15, the US has not announced any interdiction, boarding, or turnaround of a Chinese-flagged or Chinese-escorted vessel. The blockade's first 24–48 hours offered the maximum-legitimacy window to establish that precedent under the Trump declaration; the US did not use it. Combined with Dong Jun's declaration, the inference available to Beijing is that the US is not yet willing to physically confront Chinese-linked tonnage at the point of transit. That inference — whether correct or not — is itself the condition under which a miscalculation-driven Scenario D event becomes most likely. Both sides now have a plausible reading of where the enforcement line actually sits, and those readings do not match.

This is the signal pattern the escalation-tree flags as the Level 4 convergence trigger's connective tissue: not a single physical event, but an ambiguity in enforcement posture that incentivizes repeated testing until one side's real position is revealed through confrontation rather than declaration.

## Section 3: Updated Scenario Table and Pressure Clock

The April 14 addendum assessed Scenario D at 15% with the caveat that Chinese ambiguity had just resolved to the escalation column and India had been identified as a near-term catalyst. The April 15 developments — Dong Jun declaration, active sanctioned tanker transits, visible interdiction ambiguity — move Scenario D directionally higher. The assessment below is preliminary pending 48-hour confirmation of whether additional Chinese-linked vessels transit and whether the US adjusts enforcement posture in response.

| Scenario | April 13 | April 14 | April 15 (preliminary) | Key April 15 Drivers |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A: Rapid Resolution | 8% | 10% | 8% | Second US-Iran meeting, if it occurs, must now address live transit dispute on top of nuclear terms — raises the bar for any agreement |
| B: Managed Naval Standoff | 47% | 43% | 38–40% | Continued erosion; "managed standoff" requires both sides observing the enforcement line, which is now visibly ambiguous |
| C: Mine or Naval Incident | 33% | 32% | 32% | Steady; IRGC drone condition still ongoing, no new April 15 development on this vector |
| D: Great-Power Incident | 12% | 15% | 20–25% | Dong Jun declaration; sanctioned tanker transits active; Pentagon/Reuters reporting gap signals US has not yet enforced against Chinese-linked tonnage |

Brent rose 2.13% to $96.80 on April 15, remaining inside the WARN zone (>$95) defined in the agent brief. The move is consistent with structural escalation rather than a single-event spike — the market is pricing incremental Scenario D mass without yet pricing the physical-incident tail. That pattern is itself informative: a single $5–10 Brent move on a confirmed Chinese vessel interdiction would be a much larger reaction than the 2.13% observed, which suggests the market reads the Dong Jun declaration as a posture shift rather than an imminent kinetic trigger.

### The pressure clock is now compressed, not just running

The April 14 addendum anchored the pressure clock to the April 21 ceasefire expiry and noted that the absence of a second US-Iran meeting by April 18–19 would substantially increase the probability of India moving on its own timeline. That framing is now insufficient. The transit dispute is live as of April 15; any second US-Iran meeting now has to address both the nuclear terms and the enforcement-of-blockade question simultaneously. The structural position gap documented April 14 (US demands full uranium enrichment halt, facility dismantlement, and fully open Hormuz; Iran's red lines include recognized enrichment right, permanent ceasefire, and asset unfreezing) now has a third axis grafted onto it: whether the blockade applies to Chinese-linked tonnage, and if so, on what enforcement terms.

India's domestic pressure clock has not paused. The LPG shortage, labor market displacement, and OFAC waiver absence documented April 14 all continue to compound. India is no longer the leading edge of the Scenario D catalyst structure, but the secondary catalyst remains active, and a future Indian bilateral transit attempt would now occur in an environment where Chinese precedent has already tested — and arguably blurred — the interdiction line.
