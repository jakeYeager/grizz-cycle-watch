# April 24 Addendum: Curve-vs-Physical Divergence — Term-Structure and Time-Domain Stage 4 Diagnostic Signature

---

## Executive Summary

Three datapoints surfaced between April 23 and April 24 that, taken together, constitute the term-structure-domain and time-domain diagnostic signature of the Kindleberger Stage 4 state already on the record. (1) Oil executives and a former senior US energy official attending Columbia's Center on Global Energy Policy summit put on the record that the Brent and WTI futures curves are "lying" — pricing a rapid Hormuz resolution that barrel-counters and physical operators cannot locate in the operational data (Semafor, 2026; Bloomberg Opinion, 2026). (2) The Dallas Fed Q1 2026 Energy Survey — a Tier 1 Federal Reserve primary survey of 120 firms — reports that only 20% of executives expect Hormuz normalization by May, with 39% (modal) citing no earlier than August; and that the US production response is capped at +250 kb/d against the 10.1 mb/d March disruption, a gap-coverage ratio below 2.5% (Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas, 2026a). The companion Working Paper 2609 DSGE scenarios generate peak Brent of $132/bbl for a two-quarter closure and $167/bbl for a three-quarter closure (Kilian, Plante, Richter, & Zhou, 2026). (3) The April 17 single-session Brent −15% print on Iranian FM Araghchi's "completely open" declaration — reversed within 24 hours by IRGC fire on three commercial hulls — is the time-domain analog of the same mispricing pattern, now logged retroactively against the backfilled FRED series.

The term-structure read (futures curve pricing resolution the physical market cannot locate) and the time-domain read (single-session −15% on an FM statement with no operational backing) are the same Stage 4 diagnostic pattern observed on two different axes. This addendum **confirms** the Kindleberger Stage 4 state the [April 20 Pressure Clock Firing report](2026-04-20-pressure-clock-firing.md) placed on the record and the [April 21 Pressure Clock Reconfiguration](2026-04-21-pressure-clock-reconfiguration.md) addendum confirmed via the April 21 mirror-move. It **does not advance** to Stage 5. It adds (a) the curve-structure view the April 20 report did not have, and (b) the Tier 1 quantitative timeline anchor Section 10 of the parent report flagged as a forward-looking need.

Scenario probabilities on the April 21 table (A ~8–10%, B ~30–35%, C 35–40%, D ~20–25%) are unchanged. Framework phase positions are unchanged. This addendum sits parallel to the April 21 reconfiguration, not stacked on it; both anchor to the April 20 parent.

## Section 1: Exec-vs-Curve Divergence at Columbia CGEP — Term-Structure Stage 4 Signature

At a Columbia Center on Global Energy Policy summit on April 23, Kaes Van't Hof, CEO of Diamondback Energy, stated on the record: "The real problem is the back end of the curve is lying to us" (Semafor, 2026). Bob McNally, head of Rapidan Energy Group and a former senior US energy official in the Bush White House, attending the same event, added: "All the barrel-counters agree the Hormuz disruption is going to cause a severe crisis" (Semafor, 2026). A companion Bloomberg Opinion piece published the same day carried the headline "The Oil Futures Market Is Lying to Us" (Bloomberg Opinion, 2026).

### The curve structure

The curve gap the executives are naming is quantitatively specific. On April 7, Dated Brent peaked at $144.42 while prompt Brent futures settled at $109.27 — a $35.15 spread between the immediate physical market and the first deferred contract (Semafor, 2026; CME Group settlement references cited in Tier 2 analyst coverage). As of the week of April 20–24, WTI December 2026 futures trade approximately $40 below the May/June prompt — the steepest WTI and Brent backwardation since the 2022 Red Sea escalation (Semafor, 2026). Backwardation is the normal market response to an immediate supply shock; the analytically material fact is the magnitude and persistence of the gap while physical-operator expectations of resolution timing point to Q3 2026 or later (see Section 2).

### Why this is a term-structure analog of April 17

Section 2 of the April 20 parent report characterized the April 17 Brent collapse and SPX all-time-high as a textbook Stage 4 print — the headline moved the directional risk premium in equities and in oil while VIX, HY spread, and gold (the structural-volatility and credit-pricing layer) did not revise. The April 17 move was a single-session time-domain expression of the same phenomenon the April 23–24 Columbia commentary documents along the term-structure axis: the forward curve is embedding a ceasefire-narrative resolution that the physical market, barrel-counters, and credit structure cannot locate.

The term-structure and time-domain expressions are diagnostically equivalent. A price series responding to Trump-administration ceasefire headlines on the day-over-day axis and a futures curve responding to the same narrative across the months-forward axis are the same signal observed at different frequencies. In Kindleberger-model terms, this is the late Stage 4 pattern where credit-signal and price-signal dynamics decouple — except here it is physical-signal and price-signal dynamics decoupling, which is a more restrictive and therefore more diagnostic version of the same decoupling.

### Trump messaging-volatility overlay

The Semafor piece documents the current administration posture as publicly stating the crisis is "almost over" while privately pressuring US drillers to increase output (Semafor, 2026). The public "almost over" framing is the narrative the curve is following; the private "increase output" pressure reveals that the administration's own operational read does not match the public message. The addendum does not make a political claim; it notes that the curve-vs-physical divergence is directly attributable to a messaging pattern in which the official narrative is not calibrated to the underlying supply reality.

## Section 2: Dallas Fed Q1 2026 Energy Survey — Tier 1 Quantitative Timeline Anchor

Section 10 of the April 20 parent report specified a forward-looking need for a Tier 1 quantitative anchor on the "when do physical operators expect this to end" question, distinct from the April 16 IEA six-week fuel-availability horizon (which is a buffer clock, not a resolution-expectation clock) and the April 9 ACI 8–10-day paraffin clock (which is a rationing-onset clock). The Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas Q1 2026 Energy Survey, released April 23, closes this gap.

### Survey methodology and headline numbers

The Dallas Fed surveyed 120 oil and gas firms active in its district (Texas, southern New Mexico, northern Louisiana) during late March and early April 2026 (Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas, 2026a). Four analytically material results:

1. **Normalization timeline.** 20% of executives expect Hormuz normalization by May; 39% (modal) expect August; remainder expect late 2026 or later.
2. **US production response.** +250 kb/d incremental output in 2026, slightly stronger in 2027. Against the IEA-cited 10.1 mb/d March disruption, this represents gap coverage below 2.5%.
3. **Post-conflict structural cost.** $2–4/bbl permanent routing-cost premium for Persian Gulf cargoes, baked in as the new normal regardless of ceasefire.
4. **Future-disruption risk.** 48% "very likely," 38% "somewhat likely" — 86% total — expect another Hormuz-class disruption within five years.

### What the survey anchors

The survey supplies three specific framework inputs the parent report did not have:

**Executive expectation distribution.** The April 17 −15% Brent collapse and the April 23–24 curve-gap pattern are both consistent with markets pricing Scenario A (rapid resolution) or a narrow Scenario B (one quarter or less). The Dallas Fed modal expectation (39% to August) is consistent with Scenario B extended to Q3 2026, not Scenario A. Only 20% of surveyed executives — a minority — hold the view the curve is pricing.

**Hard US supply-response ceiling.** The watch list had been monitoring "Brent crude" and "US production response" qualitatively. The +250 kb/d ceiling is now quantified. At that coverage ratio (<2.5% of the 10.1 mb/d gap), the "drill more" political message documented in Section 1 does not have a physical production pathway inside the 2026 calendar year. Van't Hof's "nothing compared to the size of the issue" framing is confirmed by Federal Reserve survey data rather than remaining a single executive quote.

**DSGE scenario brackets.** Dallas Fed Working Paper 2609 — "The Impact of the 2026 Iran War on U.S. Inflation: A Scenario Analysis" (Kilian, Plante, Richter, & Zhou, 2026) — calibrates a DSGE model against the 1973 Arab-Israeli War oil shock. Output: peak Brent of $132/bbl for a two-quarter closure and $167/bbl for a three-quarter closure. Current Brent settled in the $98–103 range across April 20–23 (`data/prices.csv`; CNBC, 2026) — below the model's one-quarter-closure peak of $110/bbl. The curve is therefore pricing a regime in which closure resolves inside one quarter, while the survey's modal expectation — and all three DSGE scenarios above the one-quarter case — suggest a regime in which closure persists two or three quarters.

### Survey timing caveat

The Q1 2026 survey responses were collected in late March and early April — before the April 18–19 kinetic escalation (IRGC fire on three hulls; USS Spruance disabling of the Iran-flagged M/V Touska) and before the April 21 open-ended ceasefire extension. The Q2 2026 survey, expected July 2026, will capture the first read on whether the April 18–21 events pull executive expectations earlier (on a near-term ceasefire interpretation) or later (on the sustained kinetic-enforcement regime). The analytically honest read is that Q1 numbers bracket what industry operators thought **before** the kinetic events; current expectations could have moved in either direction since. The framework effect of the Q1 numbers holds regardless — the Stage 4 diagnostic print under examination is the April 17 move, which occurred inside the survey window and therefore cannot have been influenced by post-survey events.

## Section 3: Time-Domain Signature — April 17 as the Single-Session Analog

The April 17 Brent move was previously characterized in Section 2 of the April 20 parent report and in the April 21 reconfiguration addendum. Logging the April 17 signal into the retroactive record on April 24 closes the price-series gap and provides a direct comparison point for the term-structure read in Section 1.

### The move

On April 17, Iranian FM Seyed Abbas Araghchi declared the Strait of Hormuz "completely open" to commercial traffic for the ceasefire period, subject to coordination with Iranian maritime authorities (Al Jazeera, 2026; CNBC, 2026). Trump's prior Thursday statement that the war "should be ending pretty soon" amplified the move. Brent settled at $98.63 (`data/prices.csv`) — a −$18.00 / −15.4% move from the April 16 close of $116.63. WTI fell to approximately $83.85 (CNBC, 2026). Equities: SPX printed an all-time high; Dow recovered all Iran-war losses; Nasdaq's winning streak reached its longest since 1992.

### The 24-hour reversal

By April 18, within 24 hours, the IRGC reimposed "strict control" over the strait and fired on three commercial vessels — including an Indian-flagged ship whose crew issued the "you gave clearance, you are firing now" distress call (Reuters, 2026; Al Jazeera, 2026; India TV News, 2026). No mine-clearance corridor had been announced. No commercial insurers re-entered the market. The FM statement had no operational backing and no mechanism that would have cleared the physical obstruction the market had priced as resolved.

### Diagnostic equivalence

The April 17 print and the April 23–24 curve-gap are the same phenomenon at two time horizons. On April 17, the market priced a narrative resolution over a single session; the operational reality falsified the pricing within 24 hours. On April 23–24, the back end of the futures curve is pricing the same narrative resolution over months-forward horizons; physical operators and the Federal Reserve survey cohort say the operational reality will not support the pricing for at least one additional quarter, and the DSGE literature says a sustained closure produces $132–$167/bbl Brent rather than the $95–$105 range the curve's back end embeds.

The Kindleberger model predicts that each oscillation in Stage 4 narrows the credible-narrative window. The April 17 → April 18 reversal narrowed the single-session credible-narrative window. The persistence of the curve gap across April 23–24 while the Dallas Fed survey and Columbia CGEP commentary directly contradict it narrows the term-structure credible-narrative window in the same way — the curve is running against a Tier 1 Federal Reserve survey and against named principal-level industry commentary in a single publication week.

## Section 5: What Breaks This Read

Three conditions, any of which would invalidate or supersede the framing in this addendum.

### 1. HY spread crossing ~3.5%

Section 10 of the April 20 parent report named 3.5–4.0% HY spread as the Kindleberger Stage 5 engage threshold. As of today's `data/prices.csv` pull, HY is at 2.86% — the same level Section 2 of the parent report documented as holding through the April 17 oscillation. If HY widens above ~3.5% on top of the curve-vs-physical pattern this addendum documents, the diagnostic shifts from Stage 4 term-structure confirmation to Stage 5 onset signature. At that point the framing in this addendum is superseded and a Level 4 full-report escalation would be warranted — the curve-vs-physical divergence combined with a credit-structure break is a materially different analytical condition than either signal in isolation.

### 2. Trump messaging-whiplash compressing the curve

The April 20 report Section 2 applied the signal-quality test through the April 17 oscillation and confirmed the move was whiplash rather than structural — VIX, HY spread, and gold held, meaning the structural-volatility and credit-pricing layer did not revise. The argument in this addendum is that whiplash has now been priced into a **persistent** curve mispricing rather than dissipating. A sustained ceasefire narrative — or any Trump-administration statement interpreted by markets as a verified resolution path — could compress the curve gap within days. The curve-vs-physical divergence is durable Stage 4 only as long as the de-escalation-signal-falsification pattern (no CENTCOM safe corridor, no commercial insurer re-entry, IRGC unpredictable fire-control) remains intact. It currently is: no corridor has been declared; no insurers have re-entered; the April 19 Touska seizure and Iran's outstanding retaliation vow keep the kinetic context live.

### 3. Q2 2026 Dallas Fed Energy Survey pulling forward

Q1 2026 responses were collected in late March and early April — before the April 18–19 kinetic escalation and before the April 21 open-ended ceasefire extension. The Q2 2026 survey could pull executive expectations in either direction. If Q2 shows a material shift toward earlier normalization expectations (May / June), the Stage 4 curve-vs-physical argument loses its Tier 1 anchor and the read narrows to the Columbia CGEP exec commentary plus the April 17 time-domain print. If Q2 extends or worsens expectations (majority past August, or tail past year-end), the argument strengthens and the curve-physical gap becomes harder for the futures market to ignore — that path is consistent with the Section 3 diagnostic-equivalence argument between the April 17 single-session falsification and the current term-structure pattern.
