# Pressure Clock Firing: April 18–24 Kinetic Events, Stage 4 Textbook Print, and Alignment-Indexed Supply Shock

---

## Executive Summary

The four-section convergence forecast in the [April 15 Pressure Clock report](2026-04-15-april-21-pressure-clock.md) for resolution against the April 21 calendar date is no longer prospective. Between April 18 and April 20 it began firing, and a second set of signals that surfaced on April 20 extends the pressure window from April 21 through April 24. The convergence is now four-axis rather than three: kinetic, market, regulatory, and — newly formalized — fuel availability.

Live fire on three commercial hulls on the Iranian side (UKMTO, 2026; Reuters, 2026), a US kinetic strike and Marine boarding of the Iran-flagged M/V Touska on the US side (CENTCOM, 2026; CNBC, 2026), and an April 17 SPX/Nasdaq all-time high that reversed within 24 hours on a single diplomatic headline (Barchart/Cboe, 2026; Al Jazeera, 2026) together move three framework pillars simultaneously: Armed Conflict past the managed-standoff assumption, Market Behavior into a textbook Kindleberger Stage 4 diagnostic print, and the India pathway inside the Three Blockade Problem from a bilateral-deal catalyst to a Washington-alignment catalyst. The Warsh confirmation hearing is confirmed for April 21 with the Tillis blockade still in place (CNN Business, 2026; TheStreet, 2026). The April 21 date now anchors the Fed-independence confrontation on a fixed calendar; the kinetic escalation the earlier report anchored to the same date arrived early.

The fuel-availability axis has moved from contingency to activated administrative regime inside three IEA member economies in a single calendar month. The Netherlands activated Phase 1 of its *Landelijk Crisisplan Olie* on April 20 — the first-ever triggering of the plan since its post-2022 creation — alongside a €967m relief package (NL Times, 2026; Ministry of Economic Affairs and Climate, 2026). Italy has operated jet-fuel rationing at seven airports since April 5 under Air BP Italia's 2,000-litre cap (Bloomberg, 2026; Fortune, 2026). Australia has operated at Level 2 of its four-level National Fuel Security Plan since March 30, with Western Australia under a statewide fuel state of emergency and the Viva Energy Geelong refinery fire (April 15) cutting ~10% of domestic refining output (DCCEEW, 2026; Bloomberg, 2026; SBS News, 2026). IEA executive director Fatih Birol, in an April 16 AP exclusive from IEA Paris HQ, stated Europe has "maybe six weeks or so (of) jet fuel left" and described Hormuz as "the largest energy crisis we have ever faced" (Associated Press, 2026). The European Commission has a coordinated-measures package scheduled for April 22, followed by an informal European Council April 23–24 (European Commission Energy DG, 2026; Council of the European Union CM-2490/26, 2026).

The asymmetric-distribution pattern under those rationing plans is itself a framework signal. The April 7 Russia/China veto of Bahrain's UN Security Council resolution to safeguard Hormuz shipping (UN, 2026), combined with continued OFAC-sanctioned transit reporting (Al Jazeera, 2026) and named exemption lists covering China, Russia, Iraq, Pakistan, Malaysia, Thailand, and a subset of Indian-flagged hulls, reframes the crisis from a global supply shock into an alignment-indexed supply shock. Aligned economies — Europe, Australia, Japan-adjacent — are absorbing the bleed through statutory rationing machinery. Unaligned and selectively-aligned economies receive overt or de facto transit waivers. The framework implication is direct: Scenario D inside the Three Blockade Problem is no longer a question of whether Hormuz closes, but of which alliance bloc absorbs the consequences of selectively-applied closure.

Five framework updates are sufficiently large that this report is a standalone Level 4 analysis rather than an addendum:

1. The ceasefire expiry is pre-empted by facts on the water. April 21 remains a pressure date for the Warsh hearing but is a formality for Iran.
2. The [April 14 Addendum](2026-04-14-kindleberger-confirmed-india-scenario-d.md) framing of India as a bilateral-deal Scenario D catalyst is inverted. Iranian fire on an Indian-flagged hull forecloses the Iran-India path and substitutes an India-US alignment path.
3. European fuel buffers have moved from "indicator being monitored" to "statutory rationing regime activated" within a G7 member state, with IEA-quantified six-week aviation-fuel exhaustion on record.
4. The Australian case demonstrates the multiplier effect of the shock landing on a country that was already the only IEA member below the treaty 90-day minimum. Structural vulnerability and cyclical shock have compounded inside the same economy.
5. The [April 15 Addendum](2026-04-15-chinese-military-transit-declaration.md) interdiction ambiguity is partially resolved on one axis and, through the April 7 UNSC veto plus named exemption lists, formally asymmetric on the other. The loophole now reads narrower on the Iran-flagged axis and structurally open on the China/Russia axis as an alignment-indexed waiver rather than an ambiguity.

## Section 1: Kinetic Enforcement on Both Sides

Within 48 hours the blockade moved from declared-but-ambiguous to enforced-with-live-fire on both sides of the strait. The sequence, from the April 18–20 signal log entries:

**April 17 — Iranian "complete opening."** Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi declared the strait "completely open" for the remaining ceasefire period, subject to a coordinated route via Iranian maritime authorities (Al Jazeera, 2026). Brent plunged more than 11% on the headline to below $91/bbl; SPX and Nasdaq 100 printed all-time highs (Barchart/Cboe, 2026); the Dow notched a seven-week high.

**April 18 — IRGC reimposes "strict control."** Within 24 hours Iran reversed. The IRGC announced Hormuz was "once more" closed and warned that "approaching the Strait of Hormuz will be considered cooperation with the enemy, and any offending vessel will be targeted" (Al Jazeera, 2026; Military.com, 2026). UK Maritime Trade Operations reported two gunboats opening fire on a tanker 20 miles north of Oman without a radio challenge; Reuters confirmed at least three vessels were fired on including an Indian-flagged ship; a container ship was struck by an unknown projectile (UKMTO, 2026; NBC News, 2026; Reuters, 2026). An Indian crew's distress call — "you gave clearance, you are firing now" — was captured in the subsequent reporting (India TV News, 2026; Jerusalem Post, 2026). Windward tracked 35 outbound vessels reversing course in the 36 hours that followed (Army Recognition, 2026), including four containerships already inside the strait.

**April 19 — US kinetic strike on M/V Touska.** USS Spruance (DDG-111, guided-missile destroyer) issued repeated warnings over a six-hour standoff to the Iranian-flagged cargo vessel M/V Touska, which was transiting the north Arabian Sea toward Bandar Abbas. After crew non-compliance, Spruance directed evacuation of the engine room and fired multiple rounds into it, disabling the ship (CENTCOM, 2026; CNBC, 2026). US Marines from the 31st Marine Expeditionary Unit then rappelled from USS Tripoli (LHA-7) and boarded the vessel (Military Times, 2026; Task & Purpose, 2026). Trump publicly confirmed the seizure, stating the US "blew a hole" in the engine room (Daily Caller, 2026). The vessel and its owners are OFAC-sanctioned. Iran's formal response: "We warn that the Armed Forces of the Islamic Republic of Iran will soon respond to and retaliate against this U.S. armed piracy" (Al Jazeera, 2026; Jerusalem Post, 2026).

**April 20 — Warsh hearing confirmed.** The Senate Banking Committee confirmed the April 21 hearing for Kevin Warsh, and Senator Thom Tillis (R-NC) publicly reiterated that he will not vote to confirm Warsh until the DOJ probe into Powell concludes (CNN Business, 2026; TheStreet, 2026; Bangor Daily News, 2026). Pirro has stated she will press the investigation even if it costs Warsh the confirmation (Al Jazeera, 2026).

### What this sequence forecloses

The April 15 Pressure Clock report framed April 21 as the date against which all four sections converged. The April 15 Addendum's interdiction-ambiguity read held that the US had not yet been willing to physically confront Iran-linked tonnage during the blockade's first 24–48 hours. Both framings are now superseded by events:

- The "managed naval standoff" baseline (Scenario B) required both sides observing the enforcement line without visible kinetic contact. That condition no longer holds. The IRGC has fired on three commercial hulls including a third-country flag; the US has disabled and seized an Iran-flagged hull. These are not drone-warning or radio-challenge events — they are live-fire events against commercial and state-flagged tonnage.
- The ceasefire-expiry frame is pre-empted. April 21 as a trigger date for Iran now operates on the retaliation timing axis (Iran's public vow, April 19) rather than on a diplomatic expiry axis. The ceasefire is functionally already broken.
- The CENTCOM third-party port loophole read is narrower. The Touska was transiting toward Bandar Abbas — an Iranian port — which kept the strike inside the previously stated enforcement scope. The US has not tested a Chinese-flagged or Chinese-escorted transit; the loophole remains open on that axis.

## Section 2: April 17 SPX/Nasdaq ATH — Textbook Stage 4 Print

The [April 14 Addendum](2026-04-14-kindleberger-confirmed-india-scenario-d.md) documented the escalation-tree Stage 4 phase-shift trigger conditions fully evidenced: Goldman prime brokerage showing 7.6:1 short-to-long exposure, six consecutive weeks of net equity selling, and institutional put buying at $4,950–$5,050 SPX strikes explicitly hedging Hormuz scenarios, alongside the five-platform private credit gate cascade on the credit side. Stage 4 was confirmed as a state, not as a forecast.

The April 17 index action is an archetypal Stage 4 diagnostic *inside* the confirmed phase, not a new stage advance. The pattern is the specific signature the [Kindleberger Mania Model](../market-behavior/kindleberger-mania-model.md) places at the Stage 4 distress inflection: a durable structural crisis (Hormuz blockade, five-platform gate cascade, Powell firing threat, Warsh hearing on a fixed date) is temporarily erased from index pricing on a single unverified diplomatic headline; equity indices print new highs into the noise; the underlying conditions reassert within 24 hours; prices reverse; the headline is forgotten.

### The structural conditions did not move

The signal-quality test from the project agent brief applies: the question is not whether indices rallied but whether the structural conditions that define Stage 4 compressed during the oscillation. They did not.

Two patterns in the series matter for the Stage 4 diagnostic:

**VIX and HY spread held through the oscillation.** VIX closed at 17.48 on April 17 — below the agent-brief WARN threshold of 25 — and HY spread held at 2.83%. If the Brent-to-sub-$91 and SPX-to-ATH move on April 17 had been a genuine repricing of the Hormuz tail, both series would have compressed materially on April 17 and widened on April 18 as the IRGC reimposition reversed the headline. They did neither. The headline moved the directional risk premium in equities and in oil; the structural pricing of volatility and credit did not revise. The move was messaging whiplash, not structural deleveraging.

**HY spread held despite six-platform private credit gate stress.** The April 14 signal log entry documented $20.8bn in Q1 sector withdrawal requests across five major alternative credit platforms, with Jamie Dimon's annual letter stating private credit losses are "running higher than reported." An HY spread inside 3% through April 17 while that stress is ongoing is the exact divergence the watch list names as a late-Stage 4 indicator: institutional credit pricing and retail sentiment running in opposite directions.

### Why this is a diagnostic print rather than a new stage

The Kindleberger framework treats Stage 4 not as a single inflection but as the phase during which smart money exits, credit tightens, and the retail narrative maximizes mass exposure. A textbook Stage 4 event is one in which retail euphoria prints on a headline the institutional book does not believe and cannot be repriced through. Goldman's 7.6:1 short-to-long ratio (April 14) and CBOE put buying at $4,950–$5,050 SPX strikes are the institutional positioning that the April 17 ATH rallied into. The indexes closed at all-time highs while the institutional book was explicitly short the tail the indexes were ignoring. That is the Stage 4 divergence in its most compressed form — the divergence was already present before the event; the event supplied a clean diagnostic print.

The framework does not require new stage advancement to warrant a Level 4 report. The diagnostic print is evidence *of the confirmed state*, relevant because it forces the framework to publicly predict a reversal that then materialized within the 24-hour window the Stage 4 pattern specifies.

## Section 3: India Framing Inversion

The [April 14 Addendum](2026-04-14-kindleberger-confirmed-india-scenario-d.md) named India as the higher-probability near-term actor for testing the US interdiction line, via a bilateral transit deal with Iran. The domestic pressure mechanism — LPG black market prices at ₹3,200–4,000 per 14.2-kg cylinder (up from ₹900–1,200 baseline), 150,000+ displaced textile workers in Surat, 500,000+ impacted in Morbi, OFAC Russian-oil waiver expired April 4–5 — was documented in full. The reasoning was that a democratic government under visible, large-scale displacement pressure has strong near-term incentives to seek a bilateral Hormuz arrangement rather than wait on multilateral diplomacy.

The April 18 IRGC fire on an Indian-flagged hull — with a distress call in the public record — forecloses the bilateral-deal path. Iran firing on the very vessels the bilateral-deal frame requires cannot be coordinated with the hypothetical of Iran cooperating with Indian transit. The April 14 framing cannot survive the April 18 fact pattern.

### The substitute path: India-US alignment

Shared exposure to Iranian kinetic action creates alignment pressure in the other direction. India's MEA has raised the incident with Iran directly (NBC News, 2026; Jerusalem Post, 2026), which places New Delhi in bilateral friction with Tehran rather than in the negotiating posture the April 14 analysis specified. The LPG shortage, OFAC-waiver absence, and labor-market displacement have not been relieved; the domestic pressure clock continues to run. The pressure now pulls India toward Washington — the interdiction-order enforcer — rather than toward an independent Iran bilateral.

This is not a reversion to the [Three Blockade Problem's](../armed-conflict/three-blockade-problem.md) base case. The original scenario table did not include an India-US convergence path as a named vector. The April 18 inversion creates a new geometry that the Three Blockade Problem did not model. India is no longer the near-term Scenario D catalyst via Iran; India is now a potential force multiplier for US interdiction posture, with visible domestic consequences if Washington cannot provide energy relief through its own blockade administration.

The "Chinese/Indian vessel transit under bilateral deal" indicator — the primary Scenario D catalyst in the Three Blockade Problem's indicator table — now bifurcates cleanly: China still carries stated transit intent (Dong Jun, April 15, "open to us"); India no longer does. The Chinese axis is where the primary Scenario D catalyst resides after April 18.

## Section 4: European Fuel Clock — Strategic Reserves at the 6–10 Day Rationing Threshold

The European fuel position moved on April 20 from an indicator on the watch list to an activated statutory regime. This is the first time in the project's recorded position that a named Armed Conflict indicator — "EU aviation fuel strategic reserve drawdown rate — buffer exhaustion timeline" — transitions from forward-looking to operationally active inside a G7 economy.

### IEA Paris HQ: six weeks on record

In an Associated Press exclusive interview at IEA headquarters in Paris on April 16, executive director Fatih Birol stated that Europe has "maybe six weeks or so (of) jet fuel left" and characterized the Hormuz disruption as "the largest energy crisis we have ever faced" (Associated Press, 2026). Birol's explicit escalation path was quantified: "soon we will hear the news that some of the flights from city A to city B might be canceled as a result of lack of jet fuel." He added that if Hormuz remains closed through end of May, "many countries — especially weaker economies — will face huge challenges including high inflation and potentially slow growth or recession."

This is the first Tier 1 quantitative buffer-exhaustion statement from the agency the project's watch list names as the authoritative reserve-rate monitor. The previously indicative indicator now has a public six-week clock attached. It is bracketed on the shorter end by the ACI Europe Director General's April 9 letter to EU Transport Commissioner Tzitzikostas and Energy Commissioner Jørgensen: "if the passage through the Strait of Hormuz does not resume in any significant and stable way within the next three weeks, systemic jet fuel shortage is set to become a reality for the EU," citing that "several European states possess strategic reserves of paraffin for only eight to ten days before having to resort to rationing" (ACI Europe, 2026).

The aviation-fuel exhaustion timeline across independent primary-tier sources is now bracketed between roughly 8–10 days (paraffin strategic reserve at several member states) and 6 weeks (EU-wide aggregate), with the 3-week "systemic shortage" threshold situated between them.

### Netherlands: first-ever activation of the Landelijk Crisisplan Olie

On April 20, the Dutch cabinet activated Phase 1 ("alertering") of the *Landelijk Crisisplan Olie* — the first time the plan has ever been triggered since its creation following Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine (NL Times, 2026; Dutch Ministry of Economic Affairs and Climate, 2026, cited via NL Times). The plan is four-level: Phase 1 designates a dedicated team to monitor fuel-intensive sectors (transport, agriculture) and international stock levels; Phase 2 is "early warning" with user prioritization; Phase 3 is "alarm" enabling a state-of-emergency declaration; Phase 4 is outright crisis.

Alongside the activation, the cabinet presented a €967m relief package (€627m spending, €340m tax measures) — increasing the untaxed travel allowance, eliminating road tax for trucks, and cutting small-commercial-van vehicle tax 50% for at least six months (NL Times, 2026; Dutch News, 2026). Notably, the Dutch coalition (D66/VVD/CDA) declined Germany's fuel-tax-cut approach on targeting grounds and chose the more formal institutional response — crisis-plan activation plus targeted transport relief — rather than the lighter price-subsidy route.

Framework effect: the activation converts the Hormuz disruption from a market-pricing event into a formal administrative regime within a G7 economy. The previously speculative "European fuel rationing" scenario condition is no longer forecast; it is in progress under named statutory authority.

### Italy: first operational airport-level rationing

Italy is the first G7 economy with confirmed operational fuel rationing in effect rather than contingency planning. Air BP Italia (BP aviation division) issued emergency notices capping fuel at 2,000 litres per aircraft for non-priority short-haul flights at Milan Linate, Bologna, Venice, and Treviso starting April 5; by April 8 the list extended to Brindisi, Pescara, and Reggio Calabria, with Brindisi completely out of fuel (Bloomberg, 2026; Fortune, 2026; Il Sole 24 Ore, 2026). Priority is assigned to ambulance flights, state aircraft, and flights of more than three hours' duration. Airlines have been forced to tanker fuel from other airports, make unscheduled technical stops, reduce passenger loads, or cancel services. The root cause is the sharp drop in kerosene deliveries from the Persian Gulf since the blockade.

Italy's aggregate commercial jet-fuel supply is nominally ~7 months (per Argus/Eurostat cited in earlier trade reporting), but airports are already rationing. The binding constraint is not aggregate reserves but logistics and last-mile distribution — a distinction the "days-of-supply" headline framing tends to obscure, and which carries over directly to Germany and France, both of which hold similar aggregate reserve depths and would hit the same logistics constraint on any further delivery disruption.

### EU-level coordination: April 22–24 policy window

The Energy Union Task Force met April 10 and confirmed crude oil supplies to EU refineries remain stable but explicitly flagged jet fuel as "the primary concern" — EU refineries cover ~70% of EU jet fuel consumption with the remainder imported and Persian Gulf the dominant import source (European Commission Energy DG, 2026). The Task Force underlined "the importance of preparing for a long-term scenario." The European Commissioner urged EU states to "refrain from taking measures that may increase fuel consumption, limit the free flow of petroleum products or disincentivise EU refinery output" — i.e., coordinated demand restraint ahead of any mandatory rationing.

The Commission has scheduled a formal energy-crisis measures package for April 22, followed by an informal European Council meeting April 23–24 (Council of the European Union, 2026, document CM-2490/26). The April 21 pressure-clock window therefore extends through April 24: whichever way the ceasefire expiry resolves, a fresh EU policy baseline will be issued within 48–72 hours. The "European fuel rationing" scenario condition is no longer speculative; the institutional machinery is positioned to upgrade from voluntary coordination (current state) to more formal allocation if the April 21 window resolves toward sustained disruption.

### EC/IEA divergence as a pre-coordination signal

An April 17 Euronews framing — "EU downplays jet fuel shortage risks despite IEA warning" — captured a visible public divergence between the European Commission's severity characterization and the IEA's six-week statement. The Commission's messaging emphasizes aggregate reserves; the IEA's emphasizes operational buffer exhaustion. This intra-institutional tension is itself a framework signal: in the regulatory and coordination literature, pre-announcement divergence between a coordinating body and a specialist agency typically precedes a formal coordination upgrade rather than a stand-down. The April 22 Commission package is the scheduled event at which that divergence either closes or becomes more formal.

## Section 5: Australian Fuel Clock — Structural Vulnerability Compounding

The Australian position breaks the "this is a European problem" read of the aviation-fuel indicator. Australia is the second Five Eyes / Quad-aligned economy (after the European axis — Netherlands, Italy, Germany, France) to have formally moved from monitoring to activated national-plan authority, and the crisis has arrived on top of a pre-existing structural vulnerability rather than into a resilient reserve position. That compounding is the point.

### The pre-existing 90-day breach

Australia is the only IEA member below the treaty 90-day minimum — a continuous shortfall since 2012 (DCCEEW, 2026). As of the current crisis, reserves stand at roughly 36–39 days petrol, 29–32 days diesel, and 29–30 days jet fuel. The Hormuz disruption is therefore landing on an economy that was already structurally carrying less than half the IEA's minimum coverage. The multiplier effect is not abstract: the same external shock that gives Europe a 6-week window gives Australia a 4–5 week window on base reserves before rationing authority is reached.

### National Fuel Security Plan — Level 2 of 4

On March 30, 2026, National Cabinet adopted the four-level *National Fuel Security Plan*, with the country currently at Level 2 ("Keep Australia moving") (Minister for Resources and Northern Australia King, 2026; DCCEEW, 2026; ACT Chief Minister statement, 2026). Fuel excise was halved (−26.3 c/L) from April 1 through June 30; Export Finance Australia was empowered to backstop importers; total crisis package A$2.55bn. Level 3 (prioritization) and Level 4 (rationing, via the *Liquid Fuel Emergency Act 1984*) are drafted and publicly referenced. The Liquid Fuel Emergency Act 1984 — which provides Level 4 rationing authority — has never been triggered in Australian history; public legal commentary (Herbert Smith Freehills Kramer, 2026; Piper Alderman, 2026) explicitly flags it as "one step away."

### Western Australia and New South Wales state emergencies

Western Australia declared a statewide fuel state of emergency on April 1 under the *Fuel, Energy and Power Resources Act 1972* (effective through September 30), with compelled supply-chain disclosure (Bloomberg, 2026; SBS News, 2026). WA is also building its own 4–12 million-litre state diesel reserve — a step that reflects state-level hedging against federal coordination failure. On March 20, New South Wales Premier Chris Minns declared an energy supply emergency under the *Energy and Utilities Administration Act*, with Commonwealth Fuel Supply Taskforce coordination prioritizing regional NSW agriculture, freight, and essential services.

### Viva Energy Geelong refinery fire

On April 15 the Viva Energy Geelong refinery — which supplies ~10% of Australia's domestic refining output and more than 50% of Victoria's fuel — suffered a major fire, with 60m flames reported in the primary petrol-production units (Viva Energy Australia, 2026; Al Jazeera, 2026; SBS News, 2026). The refinery is currently operating at ~60% petrol and 80% diesel/aviation. Prime Minister Albanese secured an additional 100 million litres of diesel from Brunei and South Korea to backfill the domestic output shortfall.

The Geelong fire is the current tail risk on the Australian fuel axis: any further disruption to domestic refining while import throughput is constrained by Hormuz-dependent Asian suppliers would compress the domestic buffer materially.

### Hormuz exposure through Asian refinery feedstock

Australia's import base is not itself Hormuz-transiting, but its suppliers are. Singapore (23% of Australian fuel imports), South Korea (32%), and Malaysia (23%) are running reduced throughput — ExxonMobil Jurong at ≤50%, Singapore Refining at ~60% — because roughly 59% of the crude feedstock to Australia's top Asian refiners transits Hormuz (Australian Aviation, 2026; Bloomberg, 2026). The Australian fuel position is therefore second-order exposed to the same constraint Europe is directly exposed to, with a shallower buffer to absorb it.

### Aviation cost shock

Qantas has cut Q4 FY26 domestic capacity ~5pp and raised fares; its jet-fuel cost forecast has been raised to A$3.1–3.3bn from A$2.5bn — an A$800m fuel cost shock (Qantas disclosures via Australian Aviation, 2026; SBS News, 2026). Virgin Australia has cut ~1% of capacity through June. This is the first documented case in the project's log of major-carrier capacity reduction explicitly attributed to Hormuz-linked fuel costs.

### Framework implication

The Australian case establishes that the crisis is distributing along an alignment axis rather than a geographic one. Europe and Australia — separated by roughly 16,000 km — are both absorbing statutory rationing regimes simultaneously, while China and Russia (the two states most directly geographically adjacent to the Gulf transit corridor) are receiving overt transit waivers. The structural vulnerability Australia was already carrying before the crisis amplifies the signal: a shock that lands on an already-thin reserve regime in an aligned economy compresses the timeline by which the framework's Scenario B "managed standoff is politically stable" assumption can be sustained.

## Section 6: Asymmetric Distribution — the Waiver Pattern and What It Means for the Framework

The rationing activations in Section 4 and Section 5 are not isolated country-level responses. They are the visible half of a formalized asymmetric-distribution regime — a selectively-enforced blockade in which aligned economies absorb the shortage through statutory machinery while a named set of states receive Iran-granted or US-tolerated transit exemptions. The primary Tier 1 evidence on record is the April 7 UN Security Council vote.

### The April 7 UNSC veto

On April 7, Russia and China vetoed the Bahrain-sponsored UN Security Council resolution to safeguard Hormuz shipping, explicitly rejecting the proposed neutral-shipping framework (UN Security Council, 2026, document SC/16330; UN News, 2026). Chinese Ambassador Fu Cong rejected the draft on "root causes" grounds. This is the most consequential multilateral-governance event of the blockade period: the two permanent UNSC members whose own tonnage most benefits from a selective regime are on record preventing a neutral-shipping framework under which that regime could be constrained.

### Named beneficiaries

The named exemption set on record, via UN press records, Al Jazeera April 14 reporting on OFAC-sanctioned tankers continuing to transit, Chatham House April briefings, and wire reporting of Iranian transit-fee / exemption lists:

- China — Dong Jun military-channel declaration April 15, "The Strait of Hormuz is open to us"
- Russia — UNSC veto April 7; continued OFAC-sanctioned tanker transit (Al Jazeera, 2026)
- India — historically exempt at the shipping-fee level, though the April 18 IRGC fire on an Indian-flagged hull shows the waiver is discretionary and reversible on the shipping-target side
- Iraq, Pakistan — named in Iranian exemption lists
- Malaysia, Thailand — granted access after direct talks with Pezeshkian and the Iranian ambassador to Thailand (Tier 2–3, lower confidence on the granular list)

The sourcing structure on this entry is mixed: the China/Russia/India/Iraq/Pakistan set is supported at Tier 1 via the UN veto record and at Tier 2 via Al Jazeera and Chatham House; the Malaysia/Thailand granularity rests on Tier 2–3 reporting and is flagged as requiring primary-source confirmation.

### Alignment-indexed supply shock — the framework reframing

The thesis this structure supports is that the crisis is no longer a global supply shock. It is an alignment-indexed supply shock. The harder a state's alignment with the Western bloc, the deeper its absorption under statutory rationing authority. The closer a state's alignment to the Iran/Russia/China axis, the more exempt. The implications run through each of the project's three active frameworks:

**Armed Conflict — Three Blockade Problem.** The Scenario D column originally modelled a great-power incident arising from US-China misalignment over a Chinese-flagged Hormuz transit attempt. The asymmetric-distribution frame sharpens the geometry: Scenario D is no longer "whether Hormuz closes," because Hormuz is already selectively closed. It is "which alliance bloc absorbs the bleed while the closure is selectively applied." A US kinetic event against a Chinese-flagged vessel under the Dong Jun declaration is no longer the only Scenario D trigger. A sustained rationing regime in Europe or Australia producing political reaction — flight cancellations in the Netherlands or Italy, a Level 3 activation in Australia, a German Bundesrat vote on emergency allocation — could force the US either to confront the waiver regime (kinetic test of the Chinese axis) or to ratify it implicitly (creating alliance-internal friction between Washington and its economically bleeding allies).

**Armed Conflict — Interdiction Ambiguity.** The April 15 Addendum read the interdiction line as ambiguous because the US had not tested a Chinese transit. The April 19 Touska event resolved the ambiguity on the Iran-flagged axis via confirmed kinetic enforcement. The April 7 UNSC veto plus the named exemption lists reframe the Chinese axis: it is not ambiguous in the sense of "awaiting a test event"; it is formally asymmetric as a structural property of the crisis. The US is not confronting Chinese-linked tonnage because the multilateral-governance framework that would have authorized universal neutral-shipping enforcement was vetoed on April 7. The US unwillingness to test the Chinese axis is therefore better read as a de facto waiver rather than an operational hesitation.

**Three Blockade Problem — India pivot.** The April 18 IRGC fire on an Indian-flagged hull, set against this asymmetric-distribution backdrop, reads as evidence that India's historical shipping-level exemption is discretionary and reversible. India is structurally closer to the Western alignment end of the axis on some dimensions (Quad membership, US defense-technology cooperation) and closer to the Russia/Iran end on others (historical Russian oil imports, Iran port cooperation at Chabahar). The April 18 event — hitting India despite historical exemption — pushes India further toward the Washington axis in the near term, because the alternative (bilateral Iran deal) was just demonstrated to be unreliable at the shipping-target level. India is absorbing the shock like an aligned economy while still carrying the historical flexibility of a non-aligned one.

**Regulatory Cycle — Phase 4/5 transition.** The re-emergence of coordinated emergency-statute regimes (*Landelijk Crisisplan Olie* activated for the first time, *Fuel, Energy and Power Resources Act 1972* invoked by WA, *Liquid Fuel Emergency Act 1984* flagged as one step away) is the counter-pattern to late Phase 4 deregulation. Statutory crisis authority is being re-exercised inside the same G7 and G7-adjacent economies in which the Phase 4 deregulatory logic has been most dominant. The framework reads this as consistent with the Phase 4→5 transition signature on the crisis-management axis specifically.

### The through-line

The single thematic through-line across Sections 4, 5, and 6 is that the blockade is selective, and selection is alignment-indexed. The statutory rationing regimes activating in Europe and Australia are the absorption mechanism. The UNSC veto, named exemption lists, and untested Chinese axis are the waiver mechanism. The interdiction line reads narrower on Iran-flagged tonnage and structurally open on China-aligned tonnage — and that is not an ambiguity awaiting resolution; it is the formalized operating state.

## Section 7: Interdiction Line — Partial Resolution

The [April 15 Addendum](2026-04-15-chinese-military-transit-declaration.md) framed the interdiction line as ambiguous: the Pentagon's "no ships passed" versus Reuters' "at least three transited" was resolved by CENTCOM's clarification that the blockade applies only to vessels entering or leaving Iranian ports — a port-destination enforcement rule that Iran-linked vessels can satisfy by declaring a non-Iranian transshipment destination. The deeper signal was what CENTCOM declined to say: the US had not announced any interdiction, boarding, or turnaround of a Chinese-flagged or Chinese-escorted vessel in the blockade's first 24–48 hours. The inference available to Beijing was that the US was not yet willing to physically confront Chinese-linked tonnage. That inference — whether correct or not — was itself the condition under which a miscalculation-driven Scenario D event is most likely.

The April 19 M/V Touska event partially resolves the ambiguity on one axis. The US will use kinetic force — live rounds into an engine room, Marine boarding from USS Tripoli — against Iran-flagged tonnage transiting toward an Iranian port (Bandar Abbas). The Touska fell cleanly inside the CENTCOM port-destination enforcement rule. The event demonstrates that the declared enforcement line is operational on the Iran-flagged axis.

The Chinese axis remains untested. The CENTCOM port-destination rule would not exempt Chinese-flagged tonnage routing to an Iranian port, but the US has not faced that case. The April 15 read — that the US is not yet willing to physically confront Chinese-linked tonnage — remains intact on the Chinese axis, because no Chinese-flagged or Chinese-escorted vessel has yet attempted an Iranian-port transit for the US to confront or decline. A Chinese-flagged transit attempt toward Bandar Abbas would be the test. Dong Jun's April 15 declaration ("The Strait of Hormuz is open to us") remains the stated operational position from the Chinese side; the US response to that specific case is the open question.

The interdiction-line ambiguity now reads narrower (Iran-flagged: confirmed kinetic enforcement) and fully open (Chinese-flagged: untested). The miscalculation condition from the April 15 Addendum is unchanged on the Chinese axis, but Iran is no longer the actor generating the ambiguity; China is.

### Scenario D reframed — which bloc bleeds

The original Scenario D column in the Three Blockade Problem modelled a great-power incident arising from US confrontation with a Chinese-flagged Hormuz transit attempt. The asymmetric-distribution pattern changes the shape of the scenario. The operative mechanism behind Scenario D is no longer whether Hormuz closes; it is which bloc pays the cost of the closure being selectively applied. Three sub-paths fall inside the revised envelope:

1. **Direct US kinetic test of the Chinese axis.** A Chinese-flagged or Chinese-escorted vessel attempts an Iranian-port transit; the US must either confront it (risking the April 15 Addendum miscalculation condition) or decline (formalizing the waiver as US-tolerated). This is the original Scenario D path, still live.

2. **Alliance-internal friction under sustained asymmetric bleed.** The European or Australian rationing regime produces visible political harm — flight cancellations, Dutch Phase 2 escalation, German contingency-triggered response, Level 3 activation in Australia — while Chinese and Russian tonnage continues to move under exemption. Allied governments pressure Washington to force the enforcement line universally. Washington either escalates (converging into path 1) or refuses (creating visible alliance fracture). This sub-path did not exist in the original Three Blockade Problem framing.

3. **Iranian retaliation cascade triggering asymmetric third-party harm.** Iran's April 19 retaliation vow is against US tonnage specifically, but Iranian targeting has already shown discretionary flexibility — the April 18 Indian-flagged ship was hit despite India's historical exemption. Any retaliation event that materializes against a non-US aligned-bloc target would compound the asymmetry and accelerate paths 1 or 2.

The probability shift from 25–30% to 30–35% reflects that the Scenario D envelope now encompasses three active sub-paths rather than one.

- **Warsh hearing (April 21).** Opening statements, Tillis procedural posture, any executive-branch reaction to the Pirro probe, any Fed official statement on the Federal Reserve Act §10 "for cause" standard.
- **European Commission energy-crisis measures package (April 22).** Output tone and content — whether the package upgrades from voluntary demand-restraint coordination to a formal allocation framework, and whether it publicly closes or widens the EC/IEA severity divergence.

Germany's fuel-tax-cut route (referenced in the Dutch coalition's explicit rejection of it on targeting grounds) is a softer intervention; any German move toward statutory allocation would be a significant escalation.
- **Australian NFSP escalation from Level 2 to Level 3.** Level 3 is "prioritization"; any further refinery disruption or Asian-supplier throughput reduction is the likely trigger. A *Liquid Fuel Emergency Act 1984* activation would be the first-ever use of that statute and would place Australia ahead of the European rationing curve.
- **Further refinery disruption.** Geelong is the current tail risk. Any additional fire, outage, or throughput cut at Geelong or at Singapore ExxonMobil Jurong, Singapore Refining, or the South Korean supply chain would compress the Australian and European buffers simultaneously.
- **Shift in China or Russia posture on the waiver gap.** Any public Chinese-flagged or Chinese-escorted tonnage transit and the US response — whether kinetic, warning-only, or declined. A Russian tanker turnaround under US challenge would be equally material. Any move by Beijing or Moscow toward a coordinated Hormuz maritime-security framework would narrow the asymmetric-distribution frame; continued veto-posture behaviour would confirm it.
- **Iran retaliation form and timing.** Iran's April 19 vow is public. The next IRGC action against US naval or commercial tonnage carries a political as well as operational trigger. Timing, target, and proportionality are all open; any retaliation against a nominally-exempt third-party flag (compare the April 18 Indian-flagged hull event) would formalize the discretionary nature of the waiver regime.
- **Chinese axis test.** Any confirmed Chinese-flagged or Chinese-escorted tonnage transit and US response. The April 15 Addendum's miscalculation condition remains live on this axis; the asymmetric-distribution framing reads the Chinese axis as a formalized waiver rather than a pending test.
- **Physical Hormuz infrastructure status.** Mines, strikes on Iranian port infrastructure, formal Iranian UNCLOS closure declaration — none of the literal Level 4 immediate triggers have been met as of April 20, but the combination of April 18 and April 19 events is functionally what the escalation-tree physical-infrastructure trigger is written to detect.
- **Price series confirmation.** Brent April 14–17 backfill from FRED when the reporting lag resolves; 10-year Treasury trajectory through the April 21 hearing and the April 22–24 Council window; any "sell America" signature (simultaneous dollar, Treasury, equity weakness with gold strength) consistent with the January 2026 template.
- **HY spread.** If HY widens above ~3.5–4.0% on top of the current pattern, the interpretation shifts from Stage 4 divergence to structural deleveraging onset, which would engage the escalation-tree Kindleberger Stage 5 phase-shift trigger.
- **Fed funds futures repricing.** Any move toward pricing cuts rather than hikes would indicate markets reading the Fed-independence threat as deflationary rather than inflationary — a signal consistent with the January 2026 template's "sell America" repricing rather than a conventional geopolitical shock.
