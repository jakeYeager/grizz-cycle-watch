# April 21 Addendum: Pressure Clock Reconfiguration — Expiry Pre-empted, Geometry Preserved, Fuel Clock Now Binding

---

## Executive Summary

The April 21 pressure clock window closed without rupture and without resolution. Hours before the two-week US-Iran ceasefire was scheduled to expire, Trump announced an open-ended extension "until such time as" Iranian leaders submit a "unified proposal" to end the war with the US and Israel (CNBC, 2026; NPR, 2026; NBC News, 2026). The Warsh confirmation hearing proceeded as scheduled, with the Tillis blockade intact and Democratic questioning concentrated on a $100M undisclosed-holdings gap and Epstein-linked-fund questions (US Senate Committee on Banking, Housing, and Urban Affairs — Minority, 2026; CNN Business, 2026; CNBC, 2026). The IMO Council issued the first Tier 1 UN specialized-agency figure on the operational scale of the closure: approximately 20,000 seafarers and 2,000 vessels stranded in the Persian Gulf (IMO, 2026; UN News, 2026).

Every dimension of the [April 20 Pressure Clock Firing report](2026-04-20-pressure-clock-firing.md) is preserved — the alignment-indexed-supply-shock thesis, the Iran-granted / US-tolerated waiver geometry on the Chinese and Russian axes, the three G7 activated rationing regimes (Netherlands Phase 1, Washington state emergency, Italy airport caps), and the Kindleberger Stage 4 whiplash pattern — except the expiry-date forcing function, which is removed. The analytic problem has shifted from "what happens at the April 21 deadline" to "what breaks the open-ended standoff." The IEA's six-week fuel-availability horizon (Birol, AP, April 16, Paris HQ) and ACI Europe's 8–10-day paraffin-reserve threshold (ACI Europe, April 9) now replace the political expiry as the hard external constraint on Scenario B.

This addendum updates three framework positions — ceasefire extension, Warsh hearing record, and IMO humanitarian quantification — and issues a revised scenario table. It does not supersede the April 20 report; it narrows its window forward.

## Section 1: Ceasefire Extension — Open-Ended Truce Against the Fuel Clock

Trump's April 21 announcement converts the April 21 binary into a soft-horizon condition with no new fixed date. The announcement followed a Pakistani mediation request; Islamabad talks were delayed; Iranian state media threw Tuesday's planned meeting into doubt; and the US preconditioned sustained de-escalation on commercial shipping through Hormuz being "fully restored" (CNBC, 2026; NPR, 2026; Time, 2026). Iran's public posture — that the April 19 Touska seizure constitutes "armed piracy" requiring retaliation, and that the vessel and crew must be released "immediately" — has not moved (Al Jazeera, 2026).

### Scenario table revision

The April 20 probabilities (A ≤5%, B 25–30%, C 35–40%, D 25–30%) required a hard-expiry pathway to close Scenario A. The open-ended extension reopens a narrow A channel and reweights B without disturbing C or D in the near term:

| Scenario | April 20 | April 21 (48-hour window) | Driver |
|---|---|---|---|
| A: Rapid Resolution | ≤5% | ~8–10% | "Unified Iranian proposal" pathway formally open; near-term use unlikely given Touska posture and stalled Islamabad talks |
| B: Managed Naval Standoff | 25–30% | ~30–35% | Open-ended truce itself is a form of managed standoff; now runs against IEA/ACI fuel clock rather than political expiry |
| C: Mine or Naval Incident | 35–40% | 35–40% hold | Kinetic context unchanged (April 18 IRGC fire, April 19 Touska seizure, outstanding Iran retaliation vow) |
| D: Great-Power Incident | 25–30% | ~20–25% | Marginally eased in 48-hour window; Chinese axis still untested, asymmetric-distribution pattern intact |

### What this does not change

The April 20 report's alignment-indexed-supply-shock thesis stands on its own data — April 7 UNSC veto, named exemption lists, three G7 activated rationing regimes. The extension is a political event on the expiry axis; it does not reverse the physical asymmetry. The "managed standoff" label continues to conceal an administratively hard condition in aligned economies — which is the frame Section 3 sharpens. The Chinese axis remains untested; the [April 15 Addendum](2026-04-15-chinese-military-transit-declaration.md) miscalculation condition on that axis is unchanged.

### De-escalation signal from the watch list — still not met

The named de-escalation signal ("US CENTCOM announces verified safe corridor; commercial insurers re-enter market") has not been met. No corridor has been declared; Hormuz is characterized as "largely obstructed" across Tier 1–2 sources through April 21. The ceasefire extension is a pressure-track decompression, not a de-escalation event.

## Section 2: Warsh Hearing — Phase 5 Candidate Signal Moves from Rhetoric to Procedural Record

The April 15 Pressure Clock report specified that the Phase 5 candidate signal did not require a firing event; the threat-plus-scheduled-hearing frame was sufficient. The April 21 hearing now adds a procedural-record dimension that changes what the framework is observing but does not advance the phase transition itself.

### The three procedural facts on the record

**Warsh's independence commitment.** Warsh vowed Fed independence on the record ("I'll be an independent actor … I will not be a sock puppet") and committed to OGE-prescribed divestiture into cash-equivalent holdings (CNN, 2026; CNBC, 2026; Roll Call, 2026). Standard practice on divestiture; the analytically material fact is elsewhere.

**The $100M disclosure gap.** Democratic questioning concentrated on roughly $100M in private-fund holdings (named on the record as The Juggernaut Fund LP and THSDFS LLC), with Senator Warren pressing directly on whether the funds invest in Trump-family vehicles, China-controlled entities, money-laundering structures, or Epstein-linked financing (US Senate Committee on Banking, Housing, and Urban Affairs — Minority, 2026; C-SPAN, 2026). Warsh declined to identify the underlying holdings or answer the categorical questions. This is a late-Phase-4 regulatory-arbitrage pattern — opaque private-vehicle exposure at the institutional apex — surfacing at the Fed-chair nomination itself, analytically distinct from the retail-channel emphasis of the [April 13 Retail Capital Channel report](2026-04-13-retail-capital-channel.md) (DOL 401(k) safe harbor rule, alternatives-in-retirement-accounts).

**Tillis blockade intact.** Senator Tillis's "no" vote remains conditional on the DOJ Powell probe being dropped. The procedural bottleneck is the probe, not the financial-disclosure questions. Powell's chairmanship ends May 15; no successor can clear committee while Tillis holds and the probe continues (Roll Call, 2026; CNN Business, 2026).

### Framework effect

The regulatory cycle framework treats judicial and legislative procedure as the observable surface at which Phase 4→5 transitions either hold or break ([Regulatory Cycle Phases](../theory/regulatory-cycle-phases.md)). The April 15 signal was executive-branch rhetoric against Fed independence; the April 21 record is the same signal on a Senate Banking Committee procedural surface, with three named pressure points (Tillis conditional, $100M disclosure gap, DOJ Powell probe). The Phase 5 candidate signal is not resolved — resolution requires either Warsh withdrawal / Trump retraction (softens) or confirmation proceeding over the Tillis blockade (advances). Neither occurred on April 21.

### Forward-looking mechanic

The binding constraint on the regulatory axis is now a procedural deadlock running against a statutory clock: Powell's chairmanship ends May 15; any successor must clear committee; Tillis's bloc holds while the DOJ probe continues. The DOJ probe has no scheduled resolution date. If the probe remains live past May 15, the Fed enters an unprecedented interim-chairmanship condition inside an active Phase 5 candidate signal. That outcome is not forecast here; it is identified as the forward-looking mechanic inside which the regulatory axis now operates.

## Section 3: IMO Humanitarian Quantification — "Managed Standoff" Reframed

On April 21 the IMO Council put approximately 20,000 seafarers and 2,000 vessels on the record as stranded in the Persian Gulf — including oil and gas tankers, bulk carriers, cargo ships, and six cruise liners that transited out through a brief 24-hour April window before Iran reclosed (IMO, 2026; UN News, 2026). The Council formally called for a halt to attacks on ships in the region and urged member states to ensure continuous provision of water, food, fuel, and essentials to stranded vessels.

This is the first Tier 1 UN specialized-agency figure on the operational scale of the closure. The framework effect is specific and narrow.

### Scenario B label reframed

The April 20 report's Scenario B ("managed naval standoff") label was calibrated against a symmetric-bleed assumption that the April 20 Section 6 asymmetric-distribution analysis already partially undermined. The IMO figure sharpens the undermining on the humanitarian axis: a state in which 20,000 seafarers are held as a baseline is not a managed state in any ordinary sense. The label is retained for comparability to prior tables, but what it names is now administratively closer to the early edge of Scenario C (humanitarian incident arising from a sustained stranded-fleet condition) than to an equilibrium standoff.

### New escalation pathway — UN-system route

The IMO Council's formal call is the kind of UN-system language that typically precedes a formal closure characterization if the situation persists. The April 20 watch list did not explicitly track a UN-system escalation pathway. The pathways now warrant monitoring:

- A UN General Assembly resolution on the closure (UNGA action does not require UNSC consensus; the April 7 UNSC veto does not foreclose it)
- UN Secretary-General direct involvement — public statement, envoy, or mediation proposal
- UNCLOS-forum referral or advisory-opinion request (ITLOS or ICJ)
- An IMO Assembly extraordinary session (distinct from Council action)

### Level 4 trigger calibration

The escalation-tree Level 4 immediate trigger specifies: "Strait of Hormuz physical infrastructure struck, mined, or declared formally closed by Iran under international law." The IMO characterization and 20,000-mariner figure are **functionally meeting** the operational scale that trigger is written to detect, while **not strictly meeting** the trigger language — IMO does not have authority to declare closure, and no UNCLOS-grade Iranian declaration has been filed. This addendum does not invoke the Level 4 immediate trigger on the IMO signal alone. The escalation bar stays calibrated at the strictly-meets threshold; the functionally-meets pattern is logged and flagged for the next review cycle.

## Section 4: Stage 4 Diagnostic — Mirror-Move Confirmation

The April 21 market session produced a mirror-image of the April 17 Stage 4 textbook print documented in the April 20 report. Brent rose ~3% to $98.48 on pre-expiry ambiguity; SPX closed at 7,064.01 (−0.63%); Nasdaq Composite 24,259.96 (−0.59%); Dow 49,149.38 (−0.59%, −293 points) (CNBC, 2026; Trading Economics, 2026). The multi-session context — Nasdaq's longest winning streak since 1992 through April 20, SPX erasing 2026 losses, then a single-session give-back on the next Hormuz headline — reproduces the April 17 euphoria-then-reversal pattern in the opposite direction.

The VIX, HY-spread, and 10-year Treasury structures through the oscillation remain consistent with the Stage 4 divergence read in the April 20 report. This is diagnostic confirmation of the confirmed phase, not a structural repricing. The Kindleberger-model prediction for late Stage 4 — that each oscillation narrows the credible-narrative window — is satisfied by the April 17 / April 18 / April 21 sequence. No reclassification warranted.

## Section 5: What Breaks the Open-Ended Standoff

The April 20 "What to Watch Next" list stands. The shift in this addendum is that the April 21 calendar anchor is gone; the forward-looking question is no longer "what happens at the deadline" but "what breaks the open-ended standoff." Six candidate break conditions, ranked by proximity:

1. **IEA six-week / ACI 8–10-day paraffin clock.** The hard external constraint on Scenario B. End-May is the IEA-quantified threshold at which Europe — and by second-order exposure, Australia — runs materially into rationing. The EU Commission package (April 22) and informal European Council (April 23–24) are the first-look events at how EU-level coordination responds. Any upgrade from voluntary demand-restraint to mandatory allocation, or any additional national plan activation (Germany, France, UK, Belgium), accelerates this clock.

2. **Iran retaliation for the Touska seizure.** Outstanding public vow. Target, timing, and proportionality all open. A strike on US naval or commercial tonnage would satisfy Scenario C conditions directly. A strike on a nominally-exempt third-party flag — the April 18 Indian-flagged pattern — would formalize the discretionary-waiver frame and likely shift probability from B toward C or D.

3. **Tillis / DOJ Powell probe timing against May 15.** If the probe remains live and Tillis holds past Powell's chairmanship end, the Fed enters interim-chairmanship condition under an active Phase 5 candidate signal. Congressional procedural events (hearing reschedules, Tillis statements, DOJ status updates) inside this window are material.

4. **Chinese axis test.** The named but untested condition from the April 15 Addendum. Any confirmed Chinese-flagged or Chinese-escorted Iranian-port transit and the US response (kinetic, warning-only, or declined) converts the asymmetric-distribution frame from formalized waiver to test event. The April 20 report's Section 7 interdiction analysis remains fully applicable.

5. **Additional refinery disruption.** The Viva Energy Geelong fire (April 15) is the current tail. Any further disruption at Geelong, at Singapore ExxonMobil Jurong, Singapore Refining, or the South Korean supply chain compresses the Australian and European buffers simultaneously and accelerates break condition 1.

6. **UN-system escalation.** UNGA resolution, Secretary-General envoy, UNCLOS forum referral, or IMO extraordinary session. Lower immediate probability but structurally material — any of these routes reframes the closure from a bilateral US-Iran dispute to a multilateral governance matter and changes what "managed standoff" can mean diplomatically.

### What does not count as a break

A resumption of Islamabad talks without a verified safe corridor. A Warsh withdrawal without resolution of the DOJ probe. Brent retracing below $95 without mine-clearance progress. These are surface-level repricing events inside the open-ended truce rather than break conditions for the standoff itself.
